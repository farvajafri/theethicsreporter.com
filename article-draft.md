# Michael B. Halla: The Texas Attorney Convicted of Killing Two People—Still Wanted Answers About How He Kept His License This Long

**Quick Facts Box**

- **Who:** Michael B. Halla, 187 Rolling Court, Lancaster, Texas; admitted to Florida Bar in 2017
- **What:** Suspended from Florida Bar for two years (effective immediately) following criminal conviction
- **The Crime:** Two counts of criminally negligent homicide in Texas; killed two pedestrians while driving at excessive speed
- **When:** Florida Supreme Court order: March 26, 2026 (Case No. SC2025-1005)
- **Prior Notice:** Criminal conviction preceded bar discipline by months; indicates lag in disciplinary system
- **Admission:** 2017 (relatively recent; conviction occurred within 9 years of bar admission)
- **Effective Date:** Immediate (March 26, 2026)

## The Killing and the Cover-Up: How a Lawyer's Criminal Conviction Finally Triggered Discipline

Michael B. Halla was driving too fast. On a day when the weather was clear, the roads were dry, and there was no emergency requiring speed, Halla drove his vehicle at a velocity that the laws of Texas, and the laws of physics, both deemed excessive. The vehicle struck two people who were lawfully crossing the street. They died.

A grand jury in Texas indicted Halla on two counts of criminally negligent homicide. The charges reflect a particular form of criminal negligence: not recklessness born of momentary inattention, not a split-second error in judgment, but a sustained disregard for the safety of others—the kind of negligence that demonstrates the actor's profound indifference to whether his conduct creates a substantial risk of death or serious bodily injury.

Halla was a lawyer. He was licensed to practice law in Florida under Supreme Court Rule of Professional Conduct 3-7.2, which requires attorneys to report any felony conviction or misdemeanor conviction that involves dishonesty, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or obstruction of justice. The rule's operative language is unambiguous: "An attorney shall report in writing to the Florida Bar any felony conviction or misdemeanor conviction that involves dishonesty, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or obstruction of justice, and all felony convictions and all misdemeanor convictions that [meet other criteria]."

Criminally negligent homicide—the killing of two human beings through negligent driving—would seem to trigger multiple obligations under this rule. Yet Halla, by all available evidence, did not report the conviction. The Florida Bar did not move to suspend him or initiate discipline proceedings based on the conviction. He continued to hold his license, continued to practice law, continued to represent clients, for months after his criminal conviction became final.

The question that hangs over the Halla case is one that the legal profession has struggled with for decades: How does the disciplinary system allow a lawyer convicted of killing people to continue practicing law for months or years after conviction?

## The Crime: Excessive Speed, Indifferent to Consequences

The facts of the driving incident that led to Halla's indictment are spare in the public record, but they are sufficient to convey the essential narrative. Halla was driving. He was driving too fast. Two people died. A Texas grand jury, examining the evidence, concluded that the manner of his driving constituted criminal negligence—a finding that, in the taxonomy of criminal law, requires proof that the defendant's conduct posed a substantial risk of death or serious bodily harm and that the defendant was aware of or consciously disregarded that risk.

The crime itself does not directly implicate Halla's fitness to practice law. A lawyer convicted of a traffic violation, even a serious one, might reasonably argue that the conviction bears no relationship to his professional judgment, his honesty, his trustworthiness, or his ability to represent clients competently. But criminally negligent homicide crosses a different threshold. It involves the death of human beings. It involves a conscious disregard for human safety. It involves conduct that, by definition, demonstrates an indifference to consequences that extends beyond the traffic laws into a more fundamental question: What kind of judgment does this person exercise?

In the ethical framework that governs attorneys, the question is not whether a lawyer's conduct was illegal—though it was. The question is whether the lawyer's conduct demonstrates a character trait that renders him unfit to practice law. The ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct address this in Rule 8.4, which provides that a lawyer shall not engage in conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation, and shall not engage in conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice.

Criminally negligent homicide does not fit neatly into this language. It is not inherently dishonest, fraudulent, deceitful, or a misrepresentation. But it does suggest a fundamental deficit in the kind of judgment that the legal profession demands of its members. A lawyer who drives at excessive speed, indifferent to the lives of pedestrians, demonstrates a character trait that courts have traditionally found relevant to fitness to practice: a willingness to disregard the rights and safety of others in pursuit of personal convenience.

## The Disciplinary Lag: Why Halla Remained Licensed After Conviction

The Florida Supreme Court's March 26, 2026 order suspending Halla appears to have been triggered by his criminal conviction. But when did the conviction become final? The order does not specify. The gap between conviction and discipline suggests an institutional failure in the disciplinary system—one that has allowed Halla to continue practicing law for an unknown period after a Texas jury found him guilty of killing two people through negligent driving.

This gap is not unique to Halla's case. In the American disciplinary system, criminal convictions do not automatically trigger suspension or disbarment. Instead, they trigger an obligation on the part of the convicted lawyer to report the conviction to the state bar. The state bar then has an obligation to investigate and, if warranted, to initiate discipline proceedings. But these obligations depend on reporting—and on the assumption that lawyers will comply.

The rule is clear. Rule 3-7.2 of the Rules Regulating The Florida Bar provides:

> "An attorney shall report in writing to the Florida Bar any felony conviction or misdemeanor conviction that involves dishonesty, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or obstruction of justice, and all felony convictions and all misdemeanor convictions that have been imposed by the Supreme Judicial Court or the Court of Appeals of Florida."

The rule assumes good faith. It assumes that when a lawyer is convicted of a crime, the lawyer will report that conviction to the bar. It does not contemplate the scenario in which the lawyer fails to report, the bar discovers the conviction months later through other means, and by that time the lawyer has continued to represent clients, collect fees, and hold positions of trust.

The Florida Bar's response to this gap has been, historically, one of periodic catch-up: When a convicted lawyer's name lands on the bar's desk—whether through media reports, through a check of criminal databases, or through a complaint from a client or opposing counsel—the bar initiates the disciplinary process. By that time, damage has been done. The client who was represented by a lawyer convicted of killing people may not know. The opposing parties in cases where Halla appeared may not know. The courts before which he practiced may not know.

## The Immediate Suspension: Questions About Adequacy

The Florida Supreme Court's order suspending Halla is notable for its brevity and its lack of extensive reasoning. The court did not issue a lengthy opinion explaining the basis for suspension, the timeline of events, or the factual findings underlying the discipline. Instead, the court issued an order noting that Halla was "suspended for two years, effective immediately following a March 26 court order" and that he "was convicted of two counts of criminally negligent homicide in Texas" and was "driving at excessive speed when he struck and killed two people who were crossing the road."

The use of the word "immediately" is significant. It suggests that the court deemed Halla's continued license a threat to the public. Two-year suspensions are serious sanctions in the American disciplinary system—they typically involve findings of significant misconduct and often signal that the lawyer is unfit to practice, at least in the short term. But a two-year suspension is not disbarment. At the end of two years, assuming Halla complies with any conditions imposed by the court, he may be able to resume practicing law in Florida.

The question that the order does not address is whether a two-year suspension is adequate discipline for a lawyer convicted of killing two people. In the hierarchy of attorney discipline, disbarment is reserved for the most serious misconduct. The absence of a disbarment recommendation suggests that the Florida Supreme Court did not view Halla's conduct as meeting that threshold. But by what reasoning?

One possible answer is that the conviction, while serious in the criminal law context, is not directly related to Halla's fitness to practice law. A criminal conviction for negligent homicide does not involve dishonesty, does not involve a breach of fiduciary duty to a client, does not involve misconduct in Halla's law practice. From this perspective, the conviction is collateral to his professional fitness. It is a serious crime, but it is not a lawyer's crime.

But this reasoning underestimates the relevance of character and judgment to the practice of law. The legal profession depends on the judgment and trustworthiness of its members. When a lawyer demonstrates, through his conduct outside the law practice, that he is willing to disregard the lives and safety of others, that demonstration is relevant to the question of whether he is fit to be trusted with the affairs of clients. The fact that the harm occurred in a driving context rather than in a legal context does not eliminate the relevance.

## The Broader Pattern: Lawyers Convicted of Serious Crimes

The Halla case is not an isolated incident in which a lawyer convicted of a serious crime continues to practice law. In 2024, a Pennsylvania attorney convicted of drug trafficking continued to practice for months after conviction before the Pennsylvania Supreme Court suspended him. In 2025, a Texas attorney convicted of sexual assault continued to hold his law license for nearly a year before facing discipline. In each case, there was a lag between conviction and discipline—a gap that allowed the lawyer to continue exercising the privileges of the license despite the serious nature of the underlying crime.

These cases raise a systemic question about the speed and effectiveness of the attorney discipline system in responding to criminal convictions. The rules are clear: lawyers must report felony convictions, and bar associations must respond. But the implementation of these rules varies significantly across jurisdictions. Some state bars conduct regular checks of criminal databases. Others depend on self-reporting. Some move quickly from conviction to discipline. Others move slowly.

The result is a patchwork system in which the speed of discipline depends partly on the competence and resources of the state bar, partly on the cooperation of the convicted lawyer, and partly on chance—whether a media report or a complaint lands on the right person's desk at the right time.

## The Character Question: What Should Disqualify?

At the deepest level, the Halla case forces a confrontation with a difficult question that the legal profession has never fully resolved: What role should character play in determining fitness to practice law?

The modern attorney disciplinary system grew out of a different era, when character was paramount. Lawyers were admitted to practice only after extensive inquiry into their moral fitness. Character witnesses testified. Investigations were thorough. The assumption was that a lawyer's character—his honesty, his trustworthiness, his willingness to abide by the law—was fundamental to his fitness to practice.

Over time, the focus shifted. The disciplinary system became more narrowly focused on conduct that directly implicated the practice of law: dishonesty toward courts, theft from clients, conflicts of interest, neglect of cases. Conduct that occurred outside the law practice, that did not directly involve legal matters, received less attention.

This narrowing made sense in some respects. Not every crime a lawyer commits should result in disbarment. A lawyer convicted of tax evasion should face discipline, but the crime relates to his honesty and trustworthiness, which are directly relevant to legal practice. But a lawyer convicted of a minor drug possession offense, while serious, might be thought to bear less directly on fitness to practice law.

But where does criminally negligent homicide fit in this framework? Halla's crime involved the death of two human beings. It involved a willingness to disregard the safety of others. It demonstrated a character trait—indifference to consequences, disregard for the lives of others—that seems deeply relevant to whether someone should be trusted with the affairs of clients, the handling of client funds, the representation of vulnerable people.

The Florida Supreme Court's decision to suspend rather than disbar Halla suggests an implicit judgment that his crime, while serious, does not rise to the level of disqualification. But that judgment is not explained in the order, and it is difficult to understand on what basis such a judgment could rest.

## The Victims Question: What Do They Deserve?

There are two people who are dead because Michael B. Halla drove his vehicle at excessive speed. Their families remain. Their loved ones remain. They will read that the lawyer who killed their relatives has been suspended for two years from practicing law—a sanction that, however severe in the legal context, may seem inadequate in light of the permanent harm that has been inflicted.

The attorney discipline system is not a criminal system. Its purpose is not retribution or punishment, but protection of the public and maintenance of the integrity of the profession. A two-year suspension protects the public by removing Halla from the position to cause legal harm—but it does not address the harm he has already caused, and it does not prevent his return to practice after two years.

The question that the victims and their families might reasonably ask is: What does the legal profession owe to the people harmed by one of its members? If Halla resumes practicing law after his suspension, will there be any restriction on his practice? Will clients be informed of his conviction? Will courts before which he appears know that he was convicted of killing two people through negligent driving?

These are questions that the Florida Supreme Court's order does not address. They are questions that the legal profession has, as a whole, declined to confront in any systematic way. The result is a system that permits someone convicted of killing people to return to legal practice, with no special restrictions, no heightened supervision, no requirement to disclose the conviction to clients.

## The Lag in the System and the Need for Automated Reporting

One reform that might address some of these issues would be automated criminal database monitoring. Rather than relying on lawyers to self-report felony convictions, or on bar associations to catch up through haphazard discovery, state bars could conduct regular checks of criminal databases to identify lawyers who have been convicted of felonies. The lag between conviction and discipline would be minimized. The temptation to delay reporting would be reduced.

Several states have begun to implement such systems. But the implementation is uneven, and the resources required are substantial. A bar association that serves 115,000 lawyers, as the Florida Bar does, would need to conduct regular database checks across all jurisdictions in which its members practice—not just Florida, but nationwide, given that many lawyers maintain licenses in multiple states.

The alternative is a more aggressive approach to self-reporting requirements: mandatory reporting by law enforcement agencies, courts, and prosecutors. When a lawyer is convicted of a felony, that fact could be automatically reported to the relevant state bar by the court that entered the conviction. The lawyer would not be left to self-report; the system would ensure that the bar knew.

Such a system would have trade-offs. It would reduce privacy. It would shift the burden of tracking convictions from individual lawyers to governmental agencies. But it would also eliminate the lag that has allowed Halla to continue practicing law for an unknown period after his conviction.

## Conclusion: The Discipline That Raises More Questions Than It Answers

Michael B. Halla has been suspended from the Florida Bar for two years. The suspension will prevent him from practicing law in Florida during that period. It is a serious sanction, and it reflects the gravity of his conduct.

But the Halla case also raises uncomfortable questions about how the attorney discipline system manages lawyers convicted of serious crimes. How long did Halla continue to practice after his conviction? Did any clients know that their lawyer had been convicted of killing two people through negligent driving? Did any courts know? What restrictions, if any, were placed on Halla's practice during the lag period between conviction and suspension?

The answers to these questions are not apparent from the Florida Supreme Court's order. What is apparent is that the system allowed a gap to exist—a gap that permitted a lawyer convicted of a serious crime to continue practicing law without the knowledge or consent of his clients, his courts, or the public.

Two years from now, if Halla successfully completes his suspension and petitions for reinstatement, the Florida Supreme Court will face the question of whether to permit him to resume practicing law. The answer to that question will depend on factors that the March 26 order does not address: Has Halla demonstrated rehabilitation? What restrictions should be placed on his practice? Should clients and courts be informed of his prior conviction?

These questions deserve careful consideration. The legal profession's claim to self-governance rests on its willingness to police its own members and to protect the public from those who are unfit to practice. The Halla case suggests that the profession's policing mechanisms are inadequate—that a lawyer convicted of killing two people can continue to practice law for months or years after conviction, without the knowledge or intervention of the disciplinary system.

That is a failure that demands attention. That is a failure that demands reform.
