In the spring of 2023, a lawyer named Peter Schwartz sat in a federal courthouse in Manhattan, watching his professional reputation collapse under the weight of a decision that none of his colleagues had anticipated. He had used ChatGPT to research case law for a brief he was filing on behalf of his client, Roberto Mata, in a personal-injury claim against Avianca, the Colombian airline. The brief Schwartz submitted contained multiple fabricated citations—cases that never existed, complete with fictional docket numbers and invented judicial reasoning. Judge P. Kevin Castel, of the Southern District of New York, called a hearing. The courtroom filled with lawyers who came to witness what would become the defining cautionary tale of the AI era in law: the moment when a technology promised to democratize and improve legal work was instead transformed, through the disciplinary machinery of courts and bar associations, into a weapon against innovation itself.

When Castel issued his opinion on June 22, 2023, he imposed sanctions of five thousand dollars on Schwartz's firm and ordered the lawyers to show cause why they should not be referred to their state bar for discipline. But his language went further. The judge seemed almost wounded by the episode, as though the profession itself had been violated. "Counsel have an absolute obligation," he wrote, "to independently verify the accuracy of any AI-generated output, particularly legal citations and factual assertions, before submitting it to a court." It was, on its face, a reasonable statement—one with which few would argue. But in the months and years that followed, the ruling would be wielded not as a guardrail for responsible innovation but as a cudgel against the possibility of innovation itself. Bar associations across the country seized upon the case, issuing ethics opinions that transformed a single lawyer's mistake into a broad template for regulation. The field tightened. The walls went up. And what had seemed, just months before, like a moment of genuine technological progress in a profession notorious for its resistance to change, became something else entirely: a test case for how professional bodies could use the apparatus of ethics to defend themselves against disruption.

The legal profession has always been protective of its territory. It is, after all, one of the last remaining industries where a state-sanctioned monopoly—enforced through bar licensing, unauthorized practice of law statutes, and disciplinary rules—controls who can offer services and how those services must be delivered. This arrangement is ancient in American law, dating back to the colonial period, though the modern corporate form of the bar association did not solidify until the early twentieth century. The stated rationale has never wavered: these restrictions exist to protect the public from incompetent or unscrupulous practitioners, to ensure that only those who have studied the law and earned the public trust can dispense legal advice.

What the public rarely hears, though scholars and a growing number of federal regulators have begun to articulate it plainly, is that these rules also protect lawyers from competition. In 1983, Judge Richard Posner, writing in an antitrust case, described the American Bar Association's model rules regarding unauthorized practice of law as "an attempt to perpetuate a monopoly to the disadvantage of consumers." The observation was caustic but not novel; it was an echo of criticisms that had been building for decades, particularly from economists and civil rights advocates who pointed out that unauthorized practice restrictions disproportionately harmed low-income people seeking affordable legal help. For most of the twentieth century, however, these criticisms remained largely confined to academic papers and law review articles. Bar associations ignored them, secure in their regulatory authority and unbothered by challenges that lacked political leverage.

Then came generative AI, and with it, a genuine question: if an artificial intelligence system could help a person understand a contract, draft a simple legal document, or even—as some systems began to claim—provide initial legal analysis, who was practicing law? Was the person using the tool? The tool itself? The company that built the tool? The question was not merely technical; it went to the heart of what the legal profession had long assumed about itself. Law, the profession maintained, was a uniquely human endeavor requiring judgment, ethical discernment, and the kind of intimate client relationship that only a licensed attorney could provide. AI threatened to render this claim absurd—not because artificial intelligence could necessarily do what lawyers did as well as they did it, but because it raised the possibility that much of what lawyers did did not actually require the kind of training and licensing the profession demanded.

Instead of engaging with this question honestly, bar associations and courts chose a different path. They did not conduct empirical studies to determine whether AI assistance actually harmed clients when used responsibly. They did not establish nuanced rules that would allow legal tech innovation to proceed while maintaining meaningful safeguards. Instead, they reached for the existing arsenal of professional discipline, retrofitting it to encompass AI use in ways that were both vague and sweepingly restrictive. The Mata decision became the template. Within months, state bar associations across the country—in California, Florida, New York, Pennsylvania, Colorado, and dozens of others—issued ethics opinions that essentially transformed the management of AI risk into a near-absolute duty of lawyer liability. If a lawyer used any AI tool, for any purpose, the opinions suggested, that lawyer was responsible not just for knowing the tool's limitations but for somehow guaranteeing that it would not produce hallucinated citations, fabricated facts, or other errors.

The burden was constructed in a way that was practically impossible to meet. How could a lawyer truly verify that a machine-learning system would not generate false information? The system itself does not know when it will do so; that is the nature of its design. And yet, by 2024 and 2025, bar associations had begun invoking these vague standards to investigate and discipline lawyers who used AI tools in ways that seemed—to any reasonable observer—cautious and appropriate. In Pennsylvania, the state supreme court went further, mandating that any attorney who used AI in drafting court filings must explicitly disclose this fact to the court, a requirement that transformed AI use from a neutral tool into a mark of suspicion. Courts began treating AI-generated errors not as simple mistakes but as violations of a lawyer's duty of candor toward the tribunal, a category of misconduct that had historically been reserved for deliberately false statements.

This regulatory apparatus was not, as bar associations claimed, neutral protection for the public. It was something more calculated: a way to make AI adoption so burdensome and risky that most lawyers simply would not attempt it. By constructing near-impossible standards of verification, by invoking disciplinary machinery against lawyers who took reasonable precautions, and by embedding the requirement that AI use be disclosed to courts in ways that flagged it as suspicious, the organized bar was building a wall against the very tools that might democratize and expand access to legal services.

The most revealing moment came in December of 2025, when the Federal Trade Commission, in a letter to the Florida Supreme Court, formally endorsed the elimination of the American Bar Association's monopoly over law school accreditation. The FTC's language was spare and direct. Professional licensing rules, the agency explained, are "justified only when they advance legitimate public-health or safety interests and are narrowly tailored to address those concerns." When bar associations use their regulatory authority to restrict competition or limit market entry—as they have long done with unauthorized practice restrictions and education requirements—they harm consumers by raising costs, reducing access, and stifling innovation. The letter noted that these harms "are to be expected when states grant professional or trade associations the authority to restrict competition among themselves or limit the ability of others to enter the profession."

What the FTC was describing, in the context of legal education, was precisely what had happened with AI regulation. Bar associations had weaponized professional discipline to protect the legal profession's economic interests. The rules against unauthorized practice, the vague standards for AI use, the requirements to disclose AI assistance to courts, the investigation of lawyers who dared to experiment with new tools—all of this was dressed in the language of ethics and consumer protection. And like much professional regulation before it, it was actually about something much simpler: preserving the profession's monopoly against the threat of technological disruption.

The irony was bitter. For decades, legal aid organizations and consumer advocates had pointed out that bar restrictions on who could provide legal services were making justice inaccessible to millions of people. The access-to-justice crisis was not a theoretical problem; it was a documented reality. Americans earning below two hundred per cent of the poverty line faced legal problems at roughly the same rate as wealthier people but had access to legal help in perhaps five per cent of their cases. A study released by the American Bar Foundation in 2023 found that for every lawyer working in the legal aid sector, there were approximately fifty-nine people living in poverty who needed legal services. The math was brutal and unchangeable given the existing regulatory regime.

Then came AI, which suddenly made it possible to envision, for the first time, a way to bridge some part of that gap. An AI system trained to help people draft basic legal documents or understand their rights could reach millions of people at a cost approaching zero. Yes, such systems had limitations; yes, they could make mistakes. But they could also do what the legal profession had never managed to do in an era of scarcity: provide some meaningful assistance to those who currently had none. What bar associations faced was a choice between allowing this technology to develop under some regulatory framework and using their disciplinary machinery to strangle it in its crib.

They chose the latter. And in doing so, they revealed what had perhaps always been true: that the rules of professional ethics, while dressed in protective language, have never been primarily about the public. They have been about professional self-interest, about the preservation of a monopoly, about the capacity of established institutions to use regulatory authority to crush threats to their dominance.

In March of 2026, as this article was being completed, news emerged that the Department of Justice had proposed a new federal regulation that would allow the Attorney General to pause any state bar investigation into any current or former DOJ lawyer, no matter where that lawyer practiced or what they were accused of. The move was shocking in its directness—a clear assertion that government lawyers should be exempt from ordinary professional discipline. And yet, viewed against the background of how bar associations had weaponized ethics rules against AI, it served as a kind of mirror. If bar associations could use professional discipline as a tool of economic protection, why couldn't the federal government use it as a tool of political protection? The rules were never neutral. They were simply expressions of power.

Peter Schwartz, the lawyer sanctioned in the Mata case, became a cautionary tale deployed by every bar association and state supreme court that wanted to justify its restrictions on AI use. His mistake—trusting a new tool without adequate safeguards—was transmuted into a principle of absolute liability. For lawyers who had never used AI, the lesson was clear: do not try. For entrepreneurs and technologists who wanted to build legal tech tools, the message was equally plain: we will use our regulatory machinery to make you fail. And for the millions of Americans who might have benefited from AI-assisted legal advice or document preparation, the result was a return to the status quo ante—a legal system that served the lawyers who could afford it and abandoned everyone else to the infinite complexity of a system that explicitly required them not to understand it.

The gatekeeping that bar associations have performed for centuries had found a new weapon. And courts, by ratifying and extending these restrictions in the name of ethics, had become willing partners in its deployment. The public never understood what had happened. The profession preferred it that way.
