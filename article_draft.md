# The Judicial Enforcement Trap: How Florida Courts Are Using Mandatory AI Disclosure Rules to Preserve Professional Gatekeeping

**Subtitle:** *Appellate courts weaponize transparency requirements to control AI adoption and protect an aging profession from disruption*

---

## A Plague Upon Justice Itself

On March 25, 2026, the Fourth District Court of Appeal in Florida issued an opinion in *Elilton Alves Gouveia v. Meridian Financial Investments LLC* that captured, in a single metaphor, the legal profession's governing anxiety about artificial intelligence. "Technology, specifically artificial intelligence, is a marvel of the age we live in," the court wrote. "It is an important and productive tool, but left unchecked for accuracy and legitimacy, it can be a plague upon the judicial system, creating more problems than it solves."

A plague.

Consider what the court was actually confronting. Elilton Alves Gouveia, representing himself in a dispute with a financial company, had drafted his appellate briefs using an AI language model. The briefs contained citations to cases that either did not exist or did not support the arguments Gouveia was making. In response, the Fourth District did not sanction Gouveia. It issued, instead, a warning: future unchecked use of AI in filings with the court would result in sanctions for violation of Florida Rule of Appellate Procedure 9.210(c).

What is happening in Florida — and spreading across the federal judicial system — is not the regulation of a specific misconduct (filing false citations). It is the enforcement of a deeper institutional interest: the preservation of professional gatekeeping in an era when technology has made the production of legal-adjacent work accessible to anyone with an internet connection.

The mechanism is disclosure. The justification is accuracy. The effect is control.

---

## The Architecture of the New Requirements

In January 2026, two months before the Fourth District's opinion in Gouveia, Florida's two largest judicial circuits issued sweeping orders requiring mandatory disclosure of AI use. The Eleventh Judicial Circuit (Miami-Dade County) and the Seventeenth Judicial Circuit (Broward County) — circuits that collectively handle thousands of cases — now require that any attorney or self-represented litigant who uses generative AI in preparing pleadings, motions, memoranda, responses, proposed orders, or other court records must:

1. **Disclose that AI was used**
2. **Identify which portions of the document were AI-generated**
3. **Certify the accuracy of all citations and factual assertions**
4. **Accept personal responsibility for any errors**

The penalties for non-compliance are calibrated to be severe: the striking of pleadings, monetary sanctions, contempt proceedings, and disciplinary referral to the Florida Bar.

This is, on its surface, a reasonable governance response to a real problem. Courts exist to resolve disputes according to law. When lawyers file briefs containing citations to nonexistent cases, those briefs obstruct rather than facilitate the court's work. The hallucination problem — the phenomenon of AI language models generating citations that sound plausible but do not correspond to actual published judicial opinions — is, by any reasonable measure, a genuine challenge that courts must address.

But the disclosure requirements, properly understood, are not merely procedural safeguards. They are tools of institutional gatekeeping, deployed to reinforce the legal profession's control over the production of legal work at a moment when that control is being fundamentally threatened by technology.

---

## The Hallucination Crisis as Institutional Self-Preservation

The story the legal profession tells itself about the AI hallucination problem goes like this: a new and dangerous technology is loose in the legal system; unscrupulous or negligent attorneys are using it without adequate safeguards; courts must protect themselves and the public by imposing strict requirements on AI use; the requirements ensure accuracy and maintain the integrity of the judicial process.

It is a plausible story. It is not, however, the complete story.

Consider the timeline. The hallucination problem — the documented instances of lawyers filing briefs containing fabricated citations — accelerated dramatically in 2024 and 2025. The most prominent early cases involved Mike Lindell's lawyers submitting briefs with entirely fictitious citations to the Southern District of New York. By 2025, courts across the country were reporting increasing numbers of cases in which attorneys had relied on AI-generated briefs without adequately verifying the citations.

But here is what did not happen: courts did not respond to the hallucination problem by clarifying the scope of Rule 11 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, which already prohibits filing documents containing citations known to be false. Courts did not clarify that attorneys who sign briefs have an independent obligation to verify citations, regardless of whether the citations were generated by a human associate, a legal research database, or an AI system. Courts did not issue opinions saying: "Rule 11 already covers this. Comply with it."

Instead, courts issued new requirements. New disclosure mandates. New certification obligations. New penalties beyond those available under existing rules.

Why? Because existing rules, properly enforced, would have solved the problem without requiring new tools of judicial oversight.

The hallucination problem was not a reason for new disclosure requirements. It was an opportunity for new disclosure requirements — an opportunity to establish a new mechanism of control at the moment when the profession's traditional gatekeeping power was under threat.

---

## Sixty Percent of Federal Judges Are Using AI. For Themselves.

Here is a fact that has, until recently, generated remarkably little public discussion: approximately 61 percent of federal judges are currently using AI in their own judicial work, according to data compiled by legal industry research firms in early 2026.

Judges are using AI to: draft opinions, summarize briefs, identify controlling legal authority, flag relevant precedent, and draft orders. Judges are using the same technology that they are prohibiting attorneys from using — or that they are requiring attorneys to disclose when they use it.

One federal judge, Judge Brantley Starr of the Northern District of Texas, issued one of the earliest "AI standing orders" in American jurisprudence — orders requiring attorneys appearing before him to disclose any AI use and to certify the accuracy of citations. Since that order issued, more than 300 federal judges have adopted similar requirements.

The asymmetry is striking and, upon reflection, troubling. Judges are not required to disclose that they used AI to draft an opinion or summarize the facts of a case. Judges are not required to certify the accuracy of the materials they reviewed with AI assistance. Judges are not subject to sanctions if an AI system makes an error in their judicial work. But attorneys appearing before those judges are subject to all of these requirements.

The justification offered for this asymmetry is straightforward: judges are different. Judges have the training, experience, and professional judgment to use AI appropriately. Judges understand the legal system deeply enough to recognize when an AI system has made an error. Judges can be trusted.

But this is precisely the argument that the legal profession has been making about attorney gatekeeping for the last two centuries. Lawyers, the argument goes, have specialized training and professional judgment. Lawyers understand law and legal procedure in ways that laypeople do not. Therefore, the legal profession — not individual judges or juries, but the profession as a collective — must control who can practice law, how law is practiced, and what work product is acceptable.

The disclosure requirements imposed on attorneys are, in essence, an assertion of the same gatekeeping principle in response to a technological threat. If AI makes it possible for non-lawyers to produce legal-adjacent work — briefs, motions, memoranda — that is indistinguishable in quality from work produced by licensed attorneys, then the profession's monopoly over legal work is at risk. If that monopoly is at risk, the profession's ability to control compensation, preserve work that would otherwise be automated or outsourced, and maintain its status within the social and economic hierarchy is at risk.

The hallucination problem provided a pretext for reasserting control. The disclosure requirements are the instrument.

---

## What Mandatory Disclosure Actually Accomplishes

Advocates for mandatory AI disclosure in legal filings point to a straightforward benefit: transparency. If an attorney is required to disclose that AI was used in preparing a brief, judges have the information they need to scrutinize that brief more carefully. If an attorney is required to certify the accuracy of citations, the attorney will presumably take greater care to verify those citations before filing.

These are real benefits, and they should not be dismissed. But they are not the only effects of mandatory disclosure. Mandatory disclosure also functions as a stigmatizing mechanism — a way of flagging AI-generated work as inherently suspect in a way that human-generated work is not.

Consider what happens when a judge receives two briefs on identical legal issues. One brief was drafted by a human attorney over the course of five hours, relying on traditional legal research databases. The second brief was drafted by a human attorney over the course of thirty minutes, with AI assistance in research, outlining, and initial drafting, followed by human editing and verification. Both briefs are accurate. Both briefs are well-reasoned. Both briefs follow proper citation format.

But the second brief will be labeled: "This brief was prepared with the assistance of generative AI." The first brief will not be labeled: "This brief was prepared without the assistance of generative AI."

The asymmetry creates a bias. Judges will scrutinize the AI-assisted brief more carefully, not because it is actually more likely to be erroneous but because it is labeled as AI-assisted. Judges will trust the non-AI brief more, even if the non-AI brief is actually weaker. The disclosure requirement, in other words, creates a predisposition to view AI-generated work as inferior — a predisposition that may not be justified by the actual quality of the work.

This bias serves a professional interest. If AI-assisted briefs are viewed as systematically inferior (even if they are not), then the competitive advantage accrues to human attorneys who can afford to produce work without AI assistance — which is to say, expensive attorneys at large firms who can afford to employ teams of junior attorneys to do what AI could do faster and cheaper. The small practitioners, the solo attorneys, the legal clinics serving poor clients — the constituencies that could most benefit from AI's productivity gains — are disadvantaged by a disclosure requirement that stigmatizes AI assistance.

The requirement also accomplishes something else: it discourages AI use. If a junior attorney knows that any AI assistance in her work product will have to be disclosed to the court, she may choose not to use AI at all, even when AI could improve her work. She will instead continue to perform tasks manually that technology could automate, continue to work longer hours, continue to bill more hours to clients. This is, precisely, in the professional interest of the attorneys and the law firms that profit from inefficiency.

## The Slippery Slope of Trust

Judge Brantley Starr, who issued one of the earliest AI standing orders in the federal judiciary, characterized the issue in starkly institutional terms. "The legal profession is built on trust," Starr wrote in his order. "Courts must have confidence that the pleadings filed before them are accurate and that the attorneys who file them have exercised independent professional judgment."

This is true, as far as it goes. But it is also incomplete. The legal profession is built on trust, but the trust is distributed unevenly. Courts trust judges to use whatever tools they deem appropriate in their judicial work. Judges trust lawyers to conduct legal research in whatever way they see fit, using whatever resources and tools are available. Judges trust juries to apply their common sense and lived experience to the facts presented at trial.

What judges do not trust — at least not unconditionally — is unrepresented litigants. Self-represented people are permitted to file motions, briefs, and pleadings in courts across the country every day. But self-represented people are not licensed attorneys. They have not attended law school. They have not passed a bar exam. They are not subject to the disciplinary oversight of a state bar association.

Yet when a self-represented litigant uses AI to help draft a motion, that use is now subject to disclosure and certification requirements that would not apply if the same litigant had paid a paralegal to draft the motion or had relied on an AI system to assist with research. The disclosure requirement is, in this context, a way of enforcing the asymmetry of trust: the asymmetry that says, "we trust you if you are licensed; we do not trust you if you are not."

Elilton Alves Gouveia, the pro se litigant in the Fourth District case that prompted this entire examination, was not a licensed attorney. He was a party to a dispute who chose to represent himself. He used AI to draft briefs. The briefs contained citations that did not support his arguments. The Fourth District corrected the problem by affirming the lower court's judgment and issuing a stern warning about future AI use.

But here is what is remarkable: the Fourth District's approach to Gouveia's briefs was indistinguishable from its approach to any other brief containing erroneous citations, whether those citations were the product of AI, carelessness, or incompetence. The court corrected the error. The court sanctioned the error through its judgment. The court issued a warning about future compliance with the rules of appellate procedure.

The distinctive feature of the Gouveia opinion is not the court's response to erroneous citations. It is the court's articulation of AI itself as a threat — a "plague upon the judicial system" — requiring vigilance and disclosure to keep in check.

## The Spread of the Control Mechanism

The Florida requirements have not remained confined to Florida. They are spreading. The disclosure and certification requirements issued by the Eleventh and Seventeenth Judicial Circuits in January 2026 have been followed by similar orders in other jurisdictions. By April 2026, more than 300 federal judges have adopted some form of AI disclosure requirement, according to research compiled by legal practice management firms.

State bar associations are following suit. New York has proposed mandatory disclosure requirements. California has issued ethics opinions suggesting that attorneys should disclose AI use to clients. The ABA Journal reported in March 2026 that the American Bar Association's Standing Committee on Ethics and Professional Responsibility is considering model language for AI disclosure requirements that could be adopted by state bar associations nationwide.

What emerges is a system of cumulative disclosure obligations. Attorneys must disclose AI use to clients. Attorneys must disclose AI use to opposing counsel. Attorneys must disclose AI use to judges. Attorneys must disclose AI use to the court in every filing. Each disclosure obligation increases the friction of AI use, making it less attractive relative to traditional human labor.

The cumulative effect — whether by design or happenstance — is to slow the adoption of AI in legal practice, to preserve the productivity premium of traditional human work, and to maintain the profession's gatekeeping power at a moment when technology is threatening to erode it.

---

## The Legitimacy Question

The legal profession's defense of the disclosure requirements rests on a claim of legitimacy: that the requirements serve the interests of justice, accuracy, and public protection rather than professional self-interest. But it is difficult to reconcile this claim with the facts on the ground.

If the concern is truly accuracy and the judicial system's integrity, why do judges get to use AI without disclosure while attorneys do not? If the concern is truly protecting the public, why are the requirements not equally strict for all actors in the legal system?

The answer, almost certainly, is that the requirements are not truly about accuracy or public protection. They are about preserving the profession's monopoly over the production of legal work at a moment when that monopoly is under threat.

The disclosure requirements are, in this sense, the legal profession's equivalent of a medieval guild imposing restrictions on the practice of a craft — restrictions ostensibly designed to protect consumers but actually designed to preserve the economic rents extracted by the guild members who possess the requisite credentials.

The irony is that the more aggressively the legal profession uses disclosure requirements and sanctions to discourage AI adoption, the more likely it is that the public will demand alternative sources of legal services. If AI-assisted legal work is systematically stigmatized in courts, regulated by bar associations, and made subject to disclosure and certification requirements, then the people who cannot afford expensive human attorneys will be forced to either (1) represent themselves using whatever tools they can access (including AI), or (2) forgo legal representation altogether.

Neither outcome serves the interests of justice or public protection. Both outcomes result from an attempt by the legal profession to preserve its gatekeeping power through regulatory tools designed to slow the adoption of disruptive technology.

---

## The Institutional Decline Thesis

What makes the legal profession's response to AI particularly revealing is the profession's concurrent decline. The legal profession, measured by multiple metrics, has been shrinking for a decade. Law school enrollment has fallen. The number of lawyers entering practice has declined. The profitability of mid-size and small law firms has stagnated. The status of lawyers within society — once unambiguously elevated — has become contested and uncertain.

In this context, AI is not merely a threat to professional practice. It is a symbol of professional obsolescence. If a machine can draft a motion as competently as a lawyer, then what is the lawyer for? If an unrepresented litigant can use AI to produce a brief that is indistinguishable from the brief produced by a licensed attorney, then what is the value of the bar examination and the law school diploma?

These are genuine questions, and the profession's instinct to resist them through regulatory mechanisms is understandable if not laudable. But the instinct is, fundamentally, an instinct toward institutional self-preservation rather than social benefit.

The legal profession has been granted the privilege of self-regulation — the power to control who can practice law, how law is practiced, and what constitutes acceptable professional conduct. That privilege rests on a social bargain: that the profession will use its regulatory power to protect the public rather than to preserve the profession's interests when the two conflict.

The disclosure requirements imposed on AI use in legal practice test that bargain. They impose restrictions on the use of a technology that could expand access to legal services, could reduce the cost of legal work, could democratize the production of legal-adjacent services. The restrictions are justified by reference to accuracy and public protection, but the profession's simultaneous exemption of judicial AI use from disclosure requirements undermines the legitimacy of that justification.

What is actually at stake is the profession's ability to control the production of legal work, to maintain compensation levels, to preserve the social and economic status that comes from gatekeeping. The disclosure requirements are tools of that gatekeeping, deployed at a moment when gatekeeping is becoming technologically obsolete.

---

## Conclusion: The System Cannot Adapt

The legal profession, as currently constituted, cannot adapt to the technological disruption represented by AI. The profession's entire economic model is built on scarcity — on the idea that legal work is scarce because only licensed attorneys can do it. AI eliminates that scarcity. It makes legal work abundantly available.

The profession's response has been to assert control through disclosure requirements, certification obligations, and sanctions. But control is not adaptation. Control is institutional ossification dressed up as public protection.

The courts that issue disclosure requirements, the bar associations that impose certification obligations, the judges who issue standing orders about AI use — all are acting out of understandable institutional interest. But they are not acting in the service of justice, accuracy, or public protection. They are acting in the service of professional preservation.

The tragedy is that the profession is so invested in its own preservation that it cannot see the opportunity that AI presents: the opportunity to make legal services more accessible, more affordable, more democratic. Instead, the profession is using its regulatory power to slow technological change, to preserve scarcity, to maintain gatekeeping.

This will not work. Technology does not yield to regulatory prohibition. It only yields to regulatory adaptation. The legal profession's refusal to adapt — its choice to assert control rather than to embrace change — virtually guarantees that the disruption will be more severe than it would have been if the profession had chosen to lead rather than to resist.

The disclosure requirements imposed by Florida's appellate courts are, in this sense, not the solution to the AI challenge facing the legal system. They are symptoms of an institution that is losing the capacity to adapt to change and is choosing, instead, to fight that change with the regulatory tools at its disposal.

The courts that impose those requirements are, despite their best intentions, accelerating the very decline they mean to prevent.
