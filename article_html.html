Quick Facts

  • Who: Henry Rappa Jr., 57, of Melrose and Dennisport, Massachusetts; admitted to the Massachusetts Bar in 1994; listed as president of the Medford Community Housing Board
  • What: Charged with two counts of embezzlement by a fiduciary, two counts of larceny over $1,200, and unauthorized practice of law
  • Amount Stolen: Approximately $681,300 total across multiple clients: $660,000 from first client (September 2023), $3,200 from second client, and $18,100 from third client
  • When: September 2023 (initial embezzlement); continued unauthorized practice through December 2025; arraigned April 2, 2026, in Malden District Court
  • The Violation: Despite two separate court orders suspending him from practice (2025 and January 2026), handled a real estate closing on December 29, 2025, while under suspension
  • Bail: Set at $2,500; pleaded not guilty
  • Prosecutor: Middlesex District Attorney Marian Ryan
  • Next Hearing: Probable cause hearing scheduled for May 27, 2026
  • Bar Status: Admitted Massachusetts Bar 1994; suspended 2025 and again January 2026 by order of Supreme Judicial Court justice
  • Prior Discipline: State Board of Bar Overseers investigation triggered by client complaint in 2025
  • Sources: Boston Globe, WCVB, CBS Boston, April 2, 2026

The story of Henry Rappa Jr. begins, as so many attorney discipline cases do, with a moment of trust. In September 2023, a civil litigation client walked into Rappa's office in Melrose and handed him $660,000. The money was not for legal fees. It was for a trust account—a segregated, protected account in which attorneys hold client funds pending the resolution of the legal matter for which those funds are being held. What the client did not know—what, in fact, the client would not know for more than a year—was that Rappa had no intention of safeguarding the money. He would spend it.

Under the Massachusetts Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.15, an attorney who holds client funds in trust is under an absolute obligation to keep those funds separate from the attorney's own property, to account for the funds scrupulously, and to return the funds on demand. The rule is not complicated. It is, in fact, one of the most fundamental fiduciary obligations in the legal profession. It is also, historically, one of the most frequently violated.

Middlesex District Attorney Marian Ryan, in a press briefing on April 2, 2026, described what happened next in language that conveyed both the mechanics of the crime and the contempt with which Rappa treated his obligations: "When he was being questioned and confronted by the client, he delayed, he deflected, and he ultimately ignored not just the client, but the regulatory authorities."

The Pattern of Concealment

The embezzlement itself might have been prosecuted as a single crime—a discrete instance of misappropriation, serious but limited in scope. What transformed Rappa's conduct from a grave individual offense into a pattern of defiance was what came after: the moment when regulatory authorities caught up with him and ordered him to stop, and he continued anyway.

Following the client's complaint to the Massachusetts Board of Bar Overseers, an investigation was initiated. The investigation found sufficient evidence of misconduct to warrant disciplinary action. In 2025, the Board of Bar Overseers sought and obtained an order from a Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court justice suspending Rappa from the practice of law. The order was unambiguous: Rappa was to resign from all fiduciary roles, close all trust accounts, return client funds, and cease practicing law.

Rappa did not comply. Or more precisely, he complied minimally and selectively, while continuing to hold himself out as a practicing attorney and continuing to accept client work.

In January 2026, barely three months after the initial suspension, the Board of Bar Overseers was forced to seek a second suspension order. This time, the violation was explicit: Rappa had continued to practice law despite the explicit order prohibiting him from doing so. The Supreme Judicial Court justice issued a second suspension order, with the same terms as the first: resign, close accounts, return funds, cease practicing.

Again, Rappa did not comply.

The December Closing: Practicing While Suspended

By any measure, the most damning element of the charges against Rappa is the allegation that he handled a real estate closing on December 29, 2025—more than a month after the second suspension order was entered—knowing that he was prohibited from practicing law.

A real estate closing is not a peripheral legal activity. It is a core lawyering function. It involves drafting or reviewing closing documents, coordinating with lenders and title companies, disbursing funds, and ensuring that the transaction is properly documented. A lawyer handling a real estate closing is, by definition, practicing law. And a lawyer who was suspended from practicing law and handled a real estate closing anyway was, by definition, practicing law in violation of a court order.

The alleged details of that December closing underscore the pattern of appropriation that characterizes the case. Rappa is accused of retaining $3,200 that should have been distributed to clients, and an additional $18,100 that should have been distributed to clients or held in trust. He spent those funds.

The total alleged embezzlement, across all clients and all transactions: approximately $681,300.

The Anatomy of Defiance

What distinguishes the Rappa case from a straightforward embezzlement is the defiance that it represents. Rappa did not merely steal from clients. He stole from clients, was caught, was ordered to stop, and then continued stealing while under a court order not to practice law.

This progression has profound implications for how the legal profession understands its own capacity for self-regulation. The theory underlying attorney discipline is that the threat of professional sanctions—suspension, disbarment, the loss of one's livelihood and professional identity—will deter misconduct. But that theory assumes that the attorney being disciplined recognizes and respects the authority of the disciplinary body issuing the sanction.

Rappa's case suggests a different possibility: that some attorneys, once they have decided to exploit their clients' trust, view disciplinary orders not as commands to be obeyed but as obstacles to be ignored. The question becomes: what happens when the disciplinary system's ultimate sanction—suspension from practice—fails to stop an attorney from continuing to practice?

In Rappa's case, the answer was criminal prosecution.

The Regulatory Failure

The fact that criminal charges were necessary to address Rappa's misconduct raises uncomfortable questions about the adequacy of the disciplinary system. The Board of Bar Overseers has broad investigative authority and the power to seek suspension orders from the courts. Those orders, when entered, are enforceable through contempt proceedings. If Rappa violated a suspension order—and the allegations suggest he did—the Board of Bar Overseers could have moved for contempt, seeking to impose sanctions ranging from fines to imprisonment for violation of the court's order.

Yet it was not the Board of Bar Overseers that brought charges against Rappa. It was the Middlesex District Attorney. The transition from administrative discipline to criminal prosecution represents an implicit acknowledgment that the administrative system was insufficient to address the misconduct.

District Attorney Ryan, in her press briefing, attempted to reframe this failure as evidence of broader systemic problems: "This case is an example of a growing number of similar instances. The question is, what should you do in retaining a lawyer? Certainly, the recommendation is that you do your research. You are going to hire someone to take on a great deal of responsibility for you. You want to be certain that that is someone who is worthy of that trust, who is not the subject of any disciplinary action."

This is, ostensibly, sound advice. But it places the burden of attorney verification on the client—the party least equipped to conduct the verification and least likely to have the sophistication to interpret bar discipline databases. It also dodges the more difficult question: if the regulatory system had functioned properly, would a suspended attorney have been able to handle a real estate closing on December 29, 2025?

The Community Housing Board Connection

Rappa's biography adds another dimension to the case. He is not merely an attorney; he is listed as president of the Medford Community Housing Board. This suggests that Rappa occupied a position of public trust—that he was involved in housing policy and community development in the Boston area.

The implications of that role are troubling. If Rappa was serving as president of a community housing board while simultaneously embezzling client funds and practicing law under a suspension order, then his misconduct extends beyond individual clients to a broader institutional failure: a community organization placed its trust in someone who was, unbeknownst to the board, under court-ordered suspension for misconduct.

Whether the Medford Community Housing Board was aware of Rappa's suspension at the time he continued to serve as president is unclear from the public record. But if the board was unaware, it raises the question of disclosure: should an attorney serving in a governance role be required to disclose ongoing disciplinary proceedings to the organization he or she serves?

The Broader Pattern

District Attorney Ryan's reference to "a growing number of similar instances" warrants closer examination. According to Ryan's statement, the Middlesex District Attorney's office is "currently investigating or prosecuting more than a half-dozen" cases involving attorneys mishandling client funds. A half-dozen cases in a single district attorney's jurisdiction suggests a pattern that extends beyond Rappa.

Whatever the cause, the fact that multiple cases are being investigated simultaneously suggests that Rappa's conduct is not an aberration but a symptom of a broader problem.

The Victims' Recourse

Under Massachusetts law, victims of attorney misconduct have two potential avenues for recovering their losses. First, they can pursue direct legal action against Rappa for breach of fiduciary duty—a civil claim that could result in judgment but is only useful if Rappa has assets to satisfy the judgment. Second, they can apply to the Massachusetts Clients' Security Board, which maintains a fund designed specifically to compensate victims of attorney misconduct who cannot recover their funds directly from the offending attorney.

The Massachusetts Clients' Security Board is an important safety net, but it has limitations. The fund is finite; not all losses can be fully compensated. And the process of applying to the board and proving one's claim requires time, documentation, and often the assistance of counsel—additional burdens on people who have already been victimized once by an attorney.

The Bar Admission Question

Rappa was admitted to the Massachusetts Bar in 1994. That means he has been a licensed attorney for 32 years. His bar website presented Rappa to the public as a practicing attorney in good standing. It did not disclose that he was under suspension.

The gap between the public presentation and the regulatory reality raises questions about the adequacy of lawyer advertising disclosure requirements. When an attorney is under suspension or subject to a pending ethics investigation, should that information be publicly available in a form that prospective clients can easily access? The Massachusetts Board of Bar Overseers maintains a database of disciplinary information, but it is not prominently featured in Google search results and would require a prospective client to take the proactive step of checking.

Rappa's Defense and What Comes Next

Rappa pleaded not guilty to all charges. His attorney, Michael Callanan, declined to comment when reached by reporters. The probable cause hearing is scheduled for May 27, 2026, at which point the prosecution will need to present sufficient evidence to support a finding that probable cause exists to believe that Rappa committed the alleged crimes.

If Rappa is convicted, sentencing will occur sometime after the trial concludes—a process that, given the complexity of financial crimes, could take months or longer. The charges he faces carry a cumulative potential sentence of more than a decade in state prison.

More significant than the prison term, from the perspective of the legal profession, will be the bar discipline that will inevitably follow a conviction. A felony conviction for embezzlement and unauthorized practice of law will almost certainly result in disbarment—the permanent removal of Rappa from the practice of law.

Conclusion: The Trust Account Crisis

The Rappa case is one of a half-dozen similar cases currently being investigated or prosecuted by the Middlesex District Attorney's office. That fact alone should concern the legal profession and the state bar regulatory authorities. The trust account—the segregated account in which attorneys hold client funds—is the most sacred responsibility of the profession. When multiple attorneys in a single jurisdiction are under investigation for misappropriating trust account funds, the question is not merely whether those individual attorneys are criminals. The question is whether the system designed to prevent such crimes is adequately funded, adequately staffed, and adequately equipped to detect and punish misconduct before millions of dollars in client funds disappear.

Henry Rappa Jr. was given two opportunities to comply with court orders suspending him from practice. He disregarded both. He was given the opportunity to return client funds. He took additional client funds instead. He was given the opportunity to admit wrongdoing and seek rehabilitation. He continued to hold himself out as a practicing attorney while under a court order prohibiting him from doing so.

The question is whether the legal profession's regulatory system, when confronted with this pattern of defiance, should have acted sooner, and whether that system has the capacity to prevent similar conduct by other attorneys in the future.
