# Marcus Allen Lipham: The Madison County Attorney Who Violated Ethics Across the Board and Left Seven Clients Out Thousands

## Quick Facts

- **Who:** Marcus Allen Lipham, Madison County, Tennessee attorney
- **What:** Permanently disbarred by the Tennessee Supreme Court; ordered to pay $26,500 in restitution to seven former clients; must repay the Tennessee Lawyers' Fund for Client Protection if any restitution comes from the fund
- **When:** Disbarred effective April 1, 2026; final hearing concluded April 2026; temporary suspension imposed November 18, 2024
- **Violations:** Fourteen separate rule violations across thirteen different Tennessee Rules of Professional Conduct, including: incompetence, scope of representation, diligence, communication, unreasonable fees, confidentiality, duties to former clients, safekeeping property, terminating representation, expediting litigation, candor toward tribunal, fairness to opposing parties, and bar admission/disciplinary matters
- **The Pattern:** Nine separate disciplinary complaints documenting a pattern of deception, neglect, and financial misappropriation spanning years
- **Clients Harmed:** Scott Campbell ($3,500), Wolf Schexnider ($3,500), Robert C. McLemore ($3,000), Dawn Bennett ($2,500), Tina Mosier ($6,000), Shaun Poole ($3,500), Sha Yvonne Savov ($4,500)
- **Prior Discipline:** Public Censure (circa 2023-2024) for violations of competence, diligence, communication, fees, and safekeeping property rules
- **Monitoring Failure:** Non-compliance with Tennessee Lawyers Assistance Program monitoring agreement; failed to complete required testing and treatment
- **Key Date:** Temporary suspension November 18, 2024 (citation: Court Order, Tennessee Supreme Court)
- **Sources:** Tennessee Supreme Court Board of Professional Responsibility; WBBJ TV; Tennessee Bar Association TBJ Select; Tennessee Courts system

## A House Divided Against Itself

Marcus Allen Lipham's path to disbarment followed a trajectory that has become, in recent years, almost predictable in its arc. A lawyer whose practice fragments under the weight of his own negligence. A disciplinary system that tries, first with warnings, then with suspension, and finally with permanent removal. Clients who believed they had hired someone to protect their legal interests and instead discovered they had hired someone who would ignore them, neglect their cases, and take their money for work that was never competently performed.

But what distinguishes the Lipham case, as articulated in the Tennessee Supreme Court's order, is the sheer breadth of the ethical violations. Lipham did not merely fail to communicate with clients or charge unreasonable fees. He violated thirteen different rules of professional conduct, across nine separate complaints, in a pattern that suggested not isolated lapses but a systematic indifference to the obligations that define the legal profession.

The Tennessee Supreme Court's Board of Professional Responsibility did not use the word "reckless" in its opinion, but the implication was unmistakable: here was an attorney who treated his professional obligations as optional suggestions rather than binding constraints on his conduct.

## The Temporary Suspension That Became Permanent

The disbarment order issued in April 2026 was, in one sense, the culmination of a process that began in November 2024, when the Tennessee Supreme Court imposed a temporary suspension on Lipham. That suspension came after the Court found that Lipham "posed a threat of substantial harm to the public" and was "substantially non-compliant with a Tennessee Lawyers Assistance Program monitoring agreement."

The language is significant. A threat to public safety is the standard that permits a court to suspend an attorney without waiting for the full disciplinary process to be completed. It is a finding that the attorney's continued practice would likely result in harm to clients or the judicial system if not immediately halted. The Court made this finding in November 2024. And then it waited.

For five months, Lipham was on temporary suspension. During those five months, the disciplinary process continued. The Hearing Panel gathered evidence from nine separate complaints. It heard testimony about clients who had been left without representation, cases that had stalled without explanation, fees that had been collected without any work being performed, and communications that had been ignored until it was too late to remedy the harm.

By the time the Hearing Panel issued its final determination in April 2026, the evidence was clear. Not only did Lipham pose a threat of substantial harm; he had already caused substantial harm. Seven clients had lost money. Seven clients had been left without legal representation. And seven clients had learned, after trusting their money and their legal problems to a licensed attorney, that they should have demanded proof of his competence before hiring him.

## A Violation on Every Front

The Hearing Panel's findings list fourteen separate rule violations. To understand what Lipham did, it is necessary to understand what these rules require.

Tennessee Rule of Professional Conduct 1.1 requires that a lawyer provide competent representation. Competence is defined as possessing "the legal knowledge, skill, preparation and diligence" necessary to represent a client. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he failed to provide competent representation to multiple clients.

Rule 1.2 requires that a lawyer act within the scope of authority communicated by the client. This is the basic rule of agency: the lawyer is not the client's master, but the client's agent. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he acted outside the scope of authority communicated by his clients.

Rule 1.3 requires diligence. It is a simple requirement: once you have undertaken representation, you must pursue the matter with reasonable diligence. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he failed to act with reasonable diligence on behalf of his clients.

Rule 1.4 requires communication. A lawyer must keep a client reasonably informed about the status of a matter, explain the matter to the extent reasonably necessary to permit the client to make informed decisions, and comply with reasonable requests for information. Lipham violated this repeatedly. The Panel found that he failed to communicate with clients and failed to provide legal services after accepting fees.

Rule 1.5 addresses fees. An attorney cannot charge a fee that is illegal or clearly excessive. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he charged unreasonable fees.

Rule 1.6 concerns confidentiality. Lipham was found to have violated the confidentiality rule, though the specifics are not elaborated in the public record.

Rule 1.9 addresses duties to former clients. Even after representation is terminated, a lawyer has obligations to the former client. Lipham violated this rule.

Rule 1.15 requires that a lawyer safekeep property and funds belonging to clients or third parties. This is the rule that prevents lawyers from raiding their trust accounts or commingling client funds with personal funds. Lipham violated this. The magnitude of the violation is evidenced by the restitution order: $26,500 to seven clients, plus potential reimbursement obligations to the state fund.

Rule 1.16 addresses termination of representation. A lawyer cannot simply abandon a client. There is a process: notice must be given, work must be completed or arranged to be completed by another lawyer, papers must be turned over, and the client must be given a reasonable opportunity to retain other counsel. Lipham violated this rule. The Panel found that he failed to properly terminate representation.

Rule 3.2 requires that a lawyer expedite litigation. This is a rule designed to prevent lawyers from using delay and obstruction as tactical devices. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he failed to expedite litigation.

Rule 3.3 requires candor toward the tribunal. A lawyer must not make false statements of fact or law. Lipham violated this. The Panel found that he made false statements to courts and parties.

Rule 3.4 addresses fairness to opposing parties and counsel. A lawyer must not obstruct another party's access to evidence or use means that have no substantial purpose other than to embarrass, delay, or burden a third person. Lipham violated this rule.

Rule 8.1 addresses bar admission and disciplinary matters. When the bar asks for information in the course of an investigation, the lawyer must respond. Lipham violated this rule by failing to respond to multiple Board inquiries for information.

Rule 8.4 is the catch-all rule. It prohibits conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation, and conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice. The Panel found that Lipham violated 8.4(a), (c), and (d) — covering dishonesty, fraud, and conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice.

What emerges from this list is not a picture of a lawyer with a single area of weakness, but a picture of a lawyer who violated nearly every rule that governs the profession.

## The Clients' Stories

The restitution order identifies seven clients by name and the amount each is owed. But numbers do not capture what it means to be a client of Marcus Allen Lipham.

Scott Campbell and Wolf Schexnider each lost $3,500. For a Madison County resident, that sum might represent several months of wages. It might be the money set aside for an emergency, for a child's education, for a down payment on a house. Both men hired Lipham presumably because they had a legal problem they could not solve themselves. Instead of solving it, Lipham took their money and did nothing.

Robert C. McLemore lost $3,000. Tina Mosier lost $6,000—the largest single loss among the identified clients. Shaun Poole lost $3,500. Sha Yvonne Savov lost $4,500. All of them made the same decision: to trust a licensed attorney with their money and their legal interests. All of them were betrayed.

What did these clients hire Lipham to do? The public record does not say. The Panel's opinion does not elaborate on the specifics of each matter. But the pattern is clear enough: clients came to Lipham with problems; Lipham took their money; and Lipham did not solve the problems. In some cases, Lipham did not even attempt to solve them. In others, Lipham appeared to have started work and then abandoned it. And when clients asked what was happening with their cases, Lipham did not respond.

This is not incompetence in the strict sense—failure to do something as well as it should be done. This is abandonment. This is taking someone's money under false pretenses.

## The System's Initial Response: The Censure

Before the disbarment, there was the censure. At some point between 2023 and 2024, Marcus Allen Lipham received a Public Censure from the Tennessee Supreme Court. The censure was the system's way of saying: we see the problem, and we believe we can correct it without removing you from the profession.

The censure was based on violations of Rules 1.1 (competence), 1.3 (diligence), 1.4 (communication), 1.5(b) (fees), and 1.15 (safekeeping property and funds). In other words, the same rules he was later found to have violated in the disbarment proceeding. The censure accomplished nothing. Lipham continued to practice. Lipham continued to violate the rules. And Lipham continued to harm clients.

The progression from censure to disbarment is instructive. It reveals the limitations of the disciplinary system's graduated response approach. The assumption underlying graduated discipline is that most lawyers who receive a public reprimand will internalize the message and correct their conduct. Lipham appears to have received no such message.

## The TLAP Monitoring Failure

In November 2024, when the Tennessee Supreme Court imposed the temporary suspension, it specifically noted that Lipham was "substantially non-compliant with a Tennessee Lawyers Assistance Program monitoring agreement." TLAP—the Tennessee Lawyers Assistance Program—is designed to help lawyers with substance abuse, mental health issues, and other problems that might affect their ability to practice law.

The fact that Lipham was subject to a TLAP monitoring agreement suggests that someone—perhaps Lipham himself, perhaps his prior disciplinary case—had identified an underlying issue that required monitoring and intervention. The agreement apparently required Lipham to complete certain testing and treatment.

Lipham did not complete it. He was, in the Court's language, "substantially non-compliant." This is a term that suggests resistance, not mere failure. To be substantially non-compliant with a monitoring agreement is to signal to the system: I understand what you are asking of me, and I have chosen not to do it.

When an attorney who is already under a prior censure for ethical violations begins to resist monitoring, the escalation to temporary suspension becomes inevitable. And when an attorney under temporary suspension continues to be the subject of new complaints—nine complaints, covering fourteen rule violations—the escalation to permanent disbarment becomes inevitable as well.

## The Disciplinary Process Under Pressure

The Lipham case reveals a disciplinary system under pressure. The Tennessee Board of Professional Responsibility had to gather evidence from nine separate complaints. It had to coordinate a Hearing Panel. It had to conduct an evidentiary hearing. And it had to prepare findings and conclusions of law that would survive appellate review if Lipham chose to challenge them.

All of this took time. Lipham was not disbarred immediately. He was placed on temporary suspension in November 2024. The final disbarred order came in April 2026—a gap of five months. During those five months, Lipham was precluded from accepting new cases, but he was permitted to wind down his existing representations.

The question implicit in this timeline is: how many clients suffered additional harm during those five months? If Lipham was failing to communicate with clients before the temporary suspension, was he any more communicative after? If he was abandoning cases, was the temporary suspension an effective brake on his ability to abandon them?

The order requires that Lipham terminate all existing representations by December 18, 2024. But what does termination look like when an attorney who has already demonstrated a pattern of non-communication and neglect is the one doing the terminating?

## The Restitution: Incomplete Justice

The Tennessee Supreme Court ordered Lipham to pay $26,500 in restitution to seven former clients. It also ordered him to pay $2,216 to the Board of Professional Responsibility for the costs of the disciplinary proceeding. All of this was to be paid within 90 days of the entry of the order.

The order does not specify what happens if Lipham fails to pay. It does not specify whether the Court has the authority to enforce the restitution order through contempt proceedings or other coercive mechanisms. In practice, restitution orders against disbarred attorneys are often difficult to enforce. The attorney no longer has a law practice. The attorney's income is likely to be limited. And the attorney's incentive to pay is, to say the least, minimal.

Tennessee does maintain a Lawyers' Fund for Client Protection, which may compensate clients who suffer financial losses due to the dishonest conduct of Tennessee attorneys. But the Lipham order includes language that shifts the burden of repayment: "In the event restitution is made by the Tennessee Lawyers' Fund for Client Protection, Lipham must reimburse the fund in the same amount."

This creates a peculiar situation: if the clients are made whole through the Fund, Lipham is not discharged. He still owes the money. He still has a legal obligation, whether or not he has the ability to pay. It is a form of accountability that survives disbarment, but it is accountability without enforcement mechanisms.

## What Comes Next

Marcus Allen Lipham is permanently disbarred from the practice of law in Tennessee. Under Tennessee's rules, he is barred from re-applying for admission for a period of years. The disbarred status will follow him for the rest of his life. If he attempts to practice law in another jurisdiction, that jurisdiction will quickly discover that he was disbarred in Tennessee.

But disbarment does not answer the question of what Lipham does next. He is, after disbarment, simply a former lawyer. He cannot practice law, but he can do virtually anything else. The Court has not imposed any restrictions on his employment, his travel, or his associations. The disciplinary system has done what it is designed to do: protected the public by removing him from the profession. Beyond that, the system's power is limited.

The question of whether Lipham will pay the restitution is, in some sense, a separate matter. It is a question of his character and his willingness to make amends to the people he harmed. The legal system can require it, but the legal system cannot ensure it.

## The Broader Pattern

The Lipham case is one of hundreds of disbarment cases that occur in the United States each year. It is notable primarily for the breadth of the violations and the explicitness of the Court's findings. The Board of Professional Responsibility did not mince words: Lipham knowingly violated thirteen different rules, across nine separate complaints, in a pattern that demonstrated a systematic indifference to the ethics of the profession.

But if anything, the Lipham case understates the problem. The disciplinary system can only address the complaints it receives. If Lipham had been more careful to avoid detection—if his clients had been less willing to complain, if the Board had been less diligent in its investigation—his misconduct might have gone on indefinitely.

The clients of Marcus Allen Lipham are among the fortunate ones. They filed complaints. They cooperated with the investigation. And they were identified by the Board and the Court as people to whom restitution is owed. But for every client who complains, there may be others who do not—people who give up, people who are too embarrassed to come forward, people who do not understand that what happened to them was actionable.

The Lipham disbarment will not restore the money to the seven identified clients. It will not undo the damage to whatever legal matters they had entrusted to him. It will not give them back the time they spent waiting for responses that never came. But it will confirm, at the highest level of Tennessee's judiciary, that what Lipham did was wrong, and that the profession will not tolerate it.
