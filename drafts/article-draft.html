Quick Facts

  • Who: Pam Bondi, former U.S. Attorney General — fired by President Trump on April 2, 2026, after 14 months in office
  • What: A coalition of 68+ lawyers, former judges, and legal scholars — including two former Florida Supreme Court chief justices — is refiling an expanded ethics complaint with the Florida Bar, now that the "sitting constitutional officer" shield no longer applies
  • The Original Complaint: Filed June 2025 by Lawyers Defending American Democracy, Democracy Defenders Fund, and Lawyers for the Rule of Law; dismissed by the Florida Bar without investigation under a policy it invented for the occasion — refusing to investigate "sitting officers appointed under the U.S. Constitution"
  • The Florida Supreme Court: Refused in October 2025 to force the Bar to investigate, ruling the complainants had not shown a "clear legal right" to mandamus relief
  • The DOJ Rule: Before her firing, Bondi proposed 28 CFR Part 77 amendments giving the Attorney General power to intercept and delay state bar investigations of current and former DOJ attorneys — a rule that would have shielded her from exactly the complaint now being refiled. Over one million public comments were submitted; the comment period closed April 6, 2026
  • The Irony: Above the Law's annual bracket named Bondi the Trump administration lawyer "most needing to see their law license revoked" — and her mid-competition firing only increased voter enthusiasm
  • The Allegations: "Serious professional misconduct threatening the rule of law" — including firing or forcing resignations of DOJ lawyers who refused unethical orders, purging attorneys who worked on Trump-related cases, and weaponizing the DOJ for political retribution
  • Potential Consequence: Disbarment from the Florida Bar — Bondi's only active law license

The shield collapsed on a Thursday afternoon.

When Pam Bondi walked out of the Department of Justice on April 2, 2026 — fired by the president she had spent fourteen months serving with a devotion that exceeded what even he apparently considered useful — a legal protection that had kept her insulated from professional accountability for more than a year evaporated with the door closing behind her.

The Florida Bar had declined to investigate a 23-page ethics complaint filed against Bondi in June 2025, signed by 68 prominent lawyers including two former Florida Supreme Court chief justices. The Bar's stated reason: it does not "investigate or prosecute sitting officers appointed under the U.S. Constitution while they are in office." The Florida Supreme Court had refused to compel the Bar to act. For fourteen months, Bondi had operated with the knowledge that her federal appointment functioned as an immunity card — not from the law of the land, but from the ethics rules that govern every other lawyer in Florida.

She is no longer a sitting officer. The immunity card has been returned. And the coalition that filed the original complaint has announced it is coming back — with what South Florida defense attorney Jon May, the lead complainant, describes as a "greatly enhanced" and "significantly stronger" factual presentation that will include conduct from the entirety of Bondi's tenure.

"Absolutely," May told the Discrepancy Report when asked whether Bondi could lose her license. "It's black and white" and "overwhelming."

The story of Pam Bondi's fall from the nation's highest law enforcement office to the doorstep of bar discipline proceedings is, on its surface, a story about political hubris and institutional accountability. But read more carefully — read in the context of the legal establishment's broader relationship with the mechanisms of professional oversight — it reveals something more structural: the ethics enforcement system does not apply equally. It never has. And the people who run it have always known this.

The Shield She Built: How the DOJ Rule Was Designed to Protect Lawyers Like Her

Before she was fired, Bondi did something that, in retrospect, reads like an act of institutional self-preservation so transparent it borders on confession. On March 5, 2026, the Department of Justice published a proposed rule in the Federal Register — Docket No. OAG199, an amendment to 28 CFR Part 77 — that would allow the Attorney General to intercept state bar ethics complaints against current and former DOJ attorneys, conduct an internal review through the Office of Professional Responsibility, and request that state bar disciplinary authorities suspend their investigations until the DOJ's review was complete.

The word "former" is doing extraordinary work in that sentence. Under the proposed rule, Pam Bondi — even after being fired — would have been entitled to have the sitting Attorney General review any ethics complaint filed against her before the Florida Bar could act. The rule she proposed would have created exactly the procedural barrier she now needs.

Over one million public comments were submitted during the 30-day comment period, which closed on April 6 — four days after Bondi's firing. The overwhelming majority opposed the rule. A coalition of 129 former judges called on Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche — Bondi's interim replacement and, coincidentally, the runner-up in Above the Law's bracket of Trump administration lawyers most in need of license revocation — to withdraw the proposed rule entirely.

The rule remains pending. Its fate under Blanche or whoever permanently replaces Bondi is uncertain. But its existence illuminates a truth about the legal establishment's relationship with its own oversight mechanisms that the Bondi saga makes impossible to ignore: the people with the most power over ethics enforcement are the people most invested in ensuring that enforcement does not reach them.

The Florida Bar's Invented Exception: A Case Study in Selective Enforcement

The Florida Bar's refusal to investigate Bondi in 2025 deserves close examination, because it reveals how the legal establishment manufactures exceptions to its own rules when the target of enforcement is powerful enough to make enforcement uncomfortable.

The Bar's stated policy — that it does not investigate "sitting officers appointed under the U.S. Constitution while they are in office" — was not, at the time of its invocation, codified in any published rule of the Florida Bar. It was not a longstanding policy with a documented history. It was not the product of a formal rulemaking process. It was, as far as anyone involved in the complaint could determine, an exception that the Bar created for the specific purpose of declining to investigate Pam Bondi.

The timing tells the story. The ethics complaint was filed in June 2025. The Bar dismissed it. Jon May sought a writ of mandamus from the Florida Supreme Court, asking the court to compel the Bar to act. The court refused. Then, in December 2025, the Florida Bar's Board of Governors asked the Florida Supreme Court to formalize the exception — to approve a rule that would codify the Bar's policy of not investigating sitting federal constitutional officers.

Read that sequence carefully. The Bar first refused to investigate, citing a policy that did not exist in its written rules. When challenged, it asked the court to create the rule retroactively — to formalize the exception that it had already applied. The new rule is still pending before the Florida Supreme Court.

This is not ethics enforcement. It is ethics avoidance — the legal establishment's disciplinary machinery working not to hold a powerful lawyer accountable but to construct, in real time, the procedural barriers necessary to avoid doing so.

Now compare this treatment to what happens when ordinary attorneys face ethics complaints. When a solo practitioner in Alabama submits a brief containing AI-hallucinated citations, the court orders him to distribute a public reprimand to every client, every opposing counsel, and every presiding judge in every pending case. When a public defender in Kentucky uses an AI tool without perfectly verifying every citation, the Sixth Circuit removes him from the case, denies him all compensation, and refers him for bar discipline. When a young municipal attorney in New Orleans turns to ChatGPT to speed up his research, he is fined and forced to resign.

But when the Attorney General of the United States is accused of firing lawyers who refused to carry out unethical orders, of purging career prosecutors for their involvement in lawful investigations, of weaponizing the nation's largest law enforcement apparatus for political retribution — the Florida Bar invents a new policy to avoid looking into it.

The asymmetry is not subtle. It is the most visible illustration yet of a disciplinary system that was designed to police the powerless and accommodate the powerful.

What the Original Complaint Actually Alleged

The June 2025 ethics complaint against Bondi was not a vague political grievance. It was a 23-page document filed by experienced attorneys, co-signed by two former Florida Supreme Court chief justices — Barbara Pariente and Peggy Quince — and supported by specific factual allegations tied to documented conduct.

The complaint accused Bondi of "serious professional misconduct that threatens the rule of law and the administration of justice." It cited three principal examples:

First, the case of DOJ lawyer Erez Reuveni, who told a federal court that the deportation of Kilmar Garcia to El Salvador — despite a court order barring his removal — was the result of an "administrative error." Reuveni was subsequently suspended by the DOJ. The complaint argued that his suspension was retaliation for candor to the tribunal — that Bondi's DOJ was punishing a lawyer for doing what the Rules of Professional Conduct require: telling a court the truth.

Second, the forced resignation of a DOJ lawyer who was ordered to pursue a criminal investigation related to an EPA clean energy contract despite a lack of evidence supporting the investigation. The lawyer refused and resigned rather than carry out what she believed was an unethical directive.

Third, the dismissal of corruption charges against former New York City Mayor Eric Adams. Career prosecutors walked out of the DOJ rather than participate in what they viewed as a politically motivated decision to drop a legitimate case. The complaint argued that Bondi's DOJ ordered the dismissal not because the evidence was insufficient but because Adams had become a political ally on deportation issues.

The complaint's central argument was that Bondi, from her first day as Attorney General, created an environment in which DOJ lawyers were forced to choose between their ethical obligations under the Rules of Professional Conduct and their employment. Her Day One memo demanded "zealous advocacy" for the president's agenda and threatened discipline for anyone who "delays or impedes the Department's mission." The complaint argued this amounted to a systematic directive to subordinate ethical obligations to political loyalty — a direct violation of the professional responsibility rules that govern every lawyer in every state.

The Enhanced Complaint: What's Coming Next

The coalition is not simply refiling the original complaint. Jon May has indicated that the new filing will be "significantly revised" to include conduct that emerged during the remaining months of Bondi's tenure — conduct that, by multiple accounts, accelerated the pattern established in the original complaint.

May told reporters the revised filing could cite additional matters in which judges "threatened or initiated" contempt proceedings against DOJ lawyers, cases in which grand juries refused to indict targets of politically motivated investigations, and Bondi's handling of the Epstein files — the specific bungling that reportedly contributed to Trump's decision to fire her.

Lauren Rikleen, executive director of Lawyers Defending American Democracy, confirmed: "I think it's reasonable to assume we will be looking at activities since the time of the original filing. A lot has happened in the last six months. All of us who do this work take a lot of care and effort in how we draft these and make sure that we have a very solid basis for every allegation."

The Democracy Defenders Fund stated: "We are fully committed to holding Pam Bondi accountable for her apparent failure to comply with her professional obligations."

Both former Florida Supreme Court justices who signed the original complaint — Pariente and Quince — have confirmed they are on board with the refiled complaint. Their involvement is significant: these are not partisan operatives or advocacy organizations with political agendas. They are retired members of the state's highest court, vouching with their professional reputations for the seriousness and merit of the allegations.

The DOJ's Purge: When the Institution That Enforces Ethics Becomes the Institution That Punishes It

The conduct alleged in the Bondi complaints represents something qualitatively different from the ordinary attorney misconduct that the ethics enforcement system was designed to address. This is not a lawyer who stole client funds, neglected a case, or submitted a brief with fabricated citations. This is the nation's chief law enforcement officer allegedly creating an institutional environment in which ethical conduct by DOJ lawyers was treated as insubordination — and punished accordingly.

The Orlando Sentinel's editorial board described the pattern directly: Bondi "disgraced, demoralized and depopulated the DOJ as if she meant to destroy it." The editorial noted that career attorneys walked out over the Adams case, were purged for having prosecuted January 6 insurrectionists, and were demoted for failing to remove portraits from the Biden administration.

Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche — now acting as Bondi's interim replacement — boasted at the Conservative Political Action Conference: "There is not a single man or woman at the Department of Justice who had anything to do with those prosecutions."

He said this as if it were an accomplishment. To the legal ethics community, it was an admission: the DOJ had systematically removed every lawyer whose professional history included prosecuting conduct that, regardless of the defendant's identity, constituted federal crimes. The purge was not about competence or even ideology. It was about ensuring that no one in the building had a record of treating the law as applicable to the president and his allies.

This is where the Bondi story connects to the broader institutional pattern that this publication has documented for months. The legal establishment's ethics enforcement machinery operates with devastating efficiency against individual attorneys who use AI tools improperly, who file briefs without perfect citations, who make mistakes that embarrass courts. It operates with manufactured paralysis against the most powerful lawyers in the country — the ones whose misconduct does not embarrass a single judge but undermines the entire justice system.

The Bracket of Shame: What Above the Law's Vote Reveals

Above the Law's annual bracket challenge — in which the legal publication's readers vote on which Trump administration lawyer most deserves to lose their license — produced a result that, while unscientific, captures something real about the legal profession's mood. Pam Bondi won. Decisively. Her firing during the competition did not reduce enthusiasm for voting against her; it increased it.

As Above the Law senior editor Joe Patrice noted, Bondi's victory "carries an extra layer of meaning because Florida regulators already brushed off a robust ethics complaint by contriving an exception barring ethical investigations of 'sitting officers appointed under the U.S. Constitution while they are in office.'"

"Not a problem anymore," Patrice observed.

The bracket result is, in one sense, a joke — a lighthearted exercise by a legal media outlet with a taste for irreverence. But in another sense, it is a referendum. The legal profession, when given the opportunity to express its view of which government lawyer has done the most damage to the profession's ethical standards, chose Bondi overwhelmingly. The fact that the second-place finisher — Todd Blanche — is now running the DOJ on a temporary basis did not go unmentioned.

The Two-Tier Ethics System: Who Gets Investigated and Who Gets Shielded

The Bondi saga crystallizes a truth that the legal establishment has spent considerable institutional energy obscuring: the attorney disciplinary system does not treat all lawyers the same. It never has. The system's asymmetries run in predictable directions.

When ordinary attorneys make mistakes — especially mistakes involving AI, technology, or anything that challenges the profession's traditional methods — the system responds with escalating force. Six-figure sanctions. Mass humiliation orders. Career-ending removals. Firm-level liability. The message is unmistakable: the system has teeth, and it will use them.

When powerful lawyers in positions of institutional authority engage in conduct that the ethics rules were specifically designed to prevent — directing subordinates to violate their professional obligations, punishing lawyers for candor to courts, weaponizing prosecutorial discretion for political purposes — the system discovers exceptions. It invents new policies. It declines to investigate. It asks courts to formalize its refusal to act. It proposes federal regulations that would create permanent procedural barriers between powerful lawyers and the disciplinary authorities charged with overseeing them.

The contrast between the system's treatment of a young New Orleans municipal attorney who used ChatGPT to speed up research — fined, publicly sanctioned, forced to resign — and its treatment of the Attorney General of the United States who allegedly directed lawyers to violate their ethical obligations on a systematic basis — shielded from investigation, protected by invented policies, insulated by federal regulatory proposals — is not a coincidence. It is a feature.

The ethics enforcement system was not designed to hold the most powerful lawyers accountable. It was designed to regulate the practitioners who serve the system — not the leaders who command it. When a practitioner threatens the system's integrity through incompetence or technological disruption, the system acts decisively. When a leader threatens the system's integrity through the abuse of power, the system discovers its own limitations.

The Pending Rule and the Future of Accountability

The DOJ's proposed rule — Bondi's parting gift to every future Attorney General who might need protection from the disciplinary process — remains pending. Over one million public comments overwhelmingly opposed it. A coalition of 129 former judges has called for its withdrawal. The New York City Bar Association has condemned it as turning the McDade Amendment "on its head." The R Street Institute, Democracy Forward, and Law Forward have each filed formal oppositions.

The rule's future depends on decisions that have not yet been made: whether Blanche or his permanent successor will finalize it, whether the courts will uphold it against the McDade Amendment's unambiguous language ("to the same extent and in the same manner as other attorneys"), and whether the legal profession's organized resistance will be sufficient to stop it.

If the rule is finalized, it will create a permanent mechanism for shielding DOJ lawyers — current and former — from the same accountability that applies to every other attorney in the country. If it is withdrawn, the Bondi complaint will proceed through the Florida Bar without federal interference, and the disciplinary system will have an opportunity to demonstrate that it can hold a former Attorney General to the same standard it applies to any other lawyer.

Neither outcome is certain. What is certain is that the system's credibility depends on the answer. A profession that can sanction a solo practitioner $109,000 for AI-hallucinated citations but cannot investigate a former Attorney General for allegedly directing lawyers to violate their professional obligations is not a self-regulating profession. It is a cartel with a disciplinary committee.

What May Wants — And What the Profession Needs

Jon May, the South Florida defense attorney leading the refiled complaint, is careful about his stated objectives. "My goal was never to get her disbarred," he told the Discrepancy Report. He said he would be satisfied with a suspension or public reprimand. But he added something more significant: "My goal here is to try to make an example out of Pam Bondi" — both to deter similar conduct by the lawyers who replace her and to demonstrate that "as long as you're a member of the bar, you can be punished for your past conduct."

May's framing is precise and important. He is not seeking personal vindication or political revenge. He is testing whether the disciplinary system can do what it claims to do: hold all lawyers accountable, regardless of their position, power, or political connections.

If the Florida Bar investigates and finds that Bondi's conduct violated the Rules of Professional Conduct — and the factual record, as described in the complaint and in the months of reporting that followed, is extensive — the profession will have demonstrated that its accountability mechanisms can function even at the highest levels. If the Bar finds a new reason not to investigate — a new exception, a new procedural barrier, a new formulation of the institutional reluctance that has characterized its response to this complaint from the beginning — the profession will have confirmed what many observers already believe: that the ethics enforcement system is calibrated to punish the weak and protect the strong.

The legal profession's self-regulatory privilege — the rare and extraordinary power to police its own members, a power not granted to any other profession in American life — rests on a social bargain. The bargain is that the profession will use this power to protect the public, not to protect itself. That bargain requires the profession to demonstrate, periodically and visibly, that its accountability mechanisms apply to everyone — including the people at the top.

The Bondi complaint is one of those demonstrations. Whether the profession passes or fails the test will say more about the state of legal ethics in America than any AI sanction, any mandatory disclosure requirement, or any standing order issued by any federal judge in the country.

The shield has fallen. The complaint is being drafted. The Florida Bar, for the first time since Bondi became Attorney General, has no excuse not to act.

Whether it will act is the question that will define the profession's commitment to its own principles — or expose that commitment as a fiction maintained for the benefit of those who have never needed to worry about it.
