Christopher Kane went to work for the Mount Pleasant Central School District. At some point, he believed that his employer retaliated against him in violation of New York's Human Rights Law. He retained counsel, filed suit, and prepared to pursue his retaliation claim through the courts. Then, before he could even get his case properly served and before any court had looked at the merits of what he alleged, a Westchester County Supreme Court judge dismissed his entire complaint on procedural grounds. Justice Robert S. Ondrovic ruled that Kane had failed to serve the summons and complaint within the time frame required by civil procedure rules — and that the circumstances did not warrant an extension.

The Appellate Division, Second Department disagreed. In an October 2025 decision, a panel comprising Justices Angela G. Iannacci, Linda Christopher, Lourdes M. Ventura, and Phillip Hom reversed Justice Ondrovic's order dismissing the complaint and denied the defendants' motion to dismiss. More importantly, the Second Department granted what Ondrovic had refused: an extension of time to complete service. Christopher Kane's retaliation lawsuit against the Mount Pleasant Central School District was restored, given life again by an appellate court that found the trial judge had misapplied the very rules he cited as the basis for dismissal.

The case — docketed as Christopher Kane v. Mount Pleasant Central School District, et al., Index No. 67094/21 — illustrates a pattern that has now appeared with notable regularity in the appellate record of Justice Robert S. Ondrovic of the Westchester County Supreme Court: rulings that dismiss cases or deny relief on procedural grounds, where the appellate court finds that the applicable balancing test was not properly applied and that the dismissal was a reversible error. In Kane's case, the procedural error had profound substantive consequences: a man alleging workplace retaliation came within a hairsbreadth of having his claims extinguished not because a court found them lacking in merit, but because a judge incorrectly applied the rules governing service of process.

The Claim: Executive Law §296 and the Right to Be Free from Retaliation

Before examining the procedural dimensions of the Kane case, it is worth pausing on what was at stake substantively — on the nature of the claim that was nearly extinguished by an erroneous procedural ruling.

New York Executive Law §296, a central provision of the New York Human Rights Law, prohibits a broad range of discriminatory and retaliatory employment practices. The anti-retaliation provision of §296 makes it unlawful for an employer to discharge, expel, or otherwise discriminate against any person because that person has opposed discriminatory practices, filed a complaint, testified in a proceeding, or otherwise participated in the enforcement of the Human Rights Law's protections. It is, in plain terms, a protection against being punished for standing up against workplace discrimination.

The anti-retaliation provision of the Human Rights Law is among the most important protections in New York employment law. Its purpose is prophylactic: if employees who report or oppose discriminatory conduct can be fired or otherwise punished for doing so with legal impunity, the entire enforcement scheme for anti-discrimination law collapses. Workers will be deterred from reporting, complaining, or cooperating with investigations if they reasonably fear that doing so will cost them their jobs. The anti-retaliation provision removes that deterrent by creating a legal remedy for those who are punished for protected activity.

Christopher Kane's lawsuit against the Mount Pleasant Central School District alleged that the district violated §296 by retaliating against him. The specific facts of his underlying discrimination or protected activity complaints, the nature of the retaliation he suffered, and the details of his employment relationship with the district are matters of record in the underlying pleadings. What is publicly established through the appellate record is that Kane had a retaliation claim that, at the time Justice Ondrovic dismissed it, had not been tested on its merits — a claim that was extinguished at the procedural threshold before any court had evaluated whether his allegations, if proved, would support a judgment in his favor.

This distinction — between procedural dismissal and merits adjudication — matters enormously in the context of the Human Rights Law. A dismissal for failure to properly serve process does not tell us anything about whether an employee was actually retaliated against. It does not tell us whether the Mount Pleasant Central School District acted lawfully or unlawfully. It tells us only that the plaintiff failed to comply with service deadlines and that the trial court found insufficient reason to extend them. When that finding is itself reversed on appeal, the procedural gatekeeping falls away and the merits can finally be addressed.

CPLR 306-b: The Service Statute and Its Safety Valves

The procedural battleground in Kane v. Mount Pleasant Central School District was CPLR 306-b, a provision of New York's Civil Practice Law and Rules that governs the time within which a plaintiff must serve process after filing suit. Understanding the reversal requires a careful look at what this statute says and how New York courts have interpreted its critical safety valves.

CPLR 306-b provides that service of the summons and complaint must be made within one hundred and twenty days after filing. This is a mandatory requirement — a plaintiff who fails to serve within the 120-day window faces the risk of dismissal. The rule is designed to prevent cases from languishing on the court's docket while defendants remain unserved and uninformed of the pending litigation against them. It serves the legitimate interests of defendants in receiving timely notice and the court system's interest in managing its caseload.

But the legislature and the courts recognized from the outset that a rigid 120-day cutoff, applied mechanically without regard to circumstances, could produce unjust results. There will always be cases in which service cannot be completed within 120 days through no fault of the plaintiff — cases involving difficult-to-locate defendants, institutional obstacles, delays in obtaining necessary information, or other circumstances that make timely service impracticable. And there will be cases in which the balance of equities, even after considering a service delay, strongly favors allowing the plaintiff to proceed rather than extinguishing claims that may have merit.

In response to these concerns, CPLR 306-b authorizes courts to extend the time for service in two distinct circumstances. The first is "good cause" — the plaintiff demonstrates a specific, particularized reason why service could not be completed within the statutory period despite diligent effort. The second, and broader, ground is the "interest of justice" — a more flexible standard that permits courts to extend time based on a multi-factor balancing of equitable considerations, even in the absence of the kind of focused excuse that would satisfy the good cause standard.

The interest of justice standard is not a rubber stamp. Courts cannot simply wave a plaintiff's service failure away on vague equitable grounds. But it is a genuine safety valve that exists precisely to prevent the kind of harsh, mechanical dismissal that would occur if 306-b operated as an absolute deadline with no exceptions. New York courts, including the Court of Appeals, have made clear that the interest of justice extension requires courts to engage in an actual balancing analysis — to weigh the multiple factors that bear on whether extending time is equitable in the specific case before them.

The Factors Courts Must Consider

The leading New York authority on the interest of justice extension under CPLR 306-b is the Court of Appeals' decision in Leader v. Maroney, Ponzini & Spencer, 97 NY2d 95 (2001). In Leader, the Court of Appeals set out the framework that all New York trial and appellate courts are required to apply when a plaintiff moves for an extension of time to serve process under the interest of justice standard.

The Leader factors are multifaceted and require holistic consideration. Courts are instructed to examine: whether the statute of limitations has expired; whether the plaintiff has been diligent in attempting to serve process; whether the defendant has been put on notice of the action; whether the defendant would be prejudiced by the extension; the merits of the underlying claim; and any other relevant circumstances that bear on whether extending time serves the interest of justice. Critically, no single factor is necessarily dispositive — the court must engage with the full picture and make a reasoned judgment.

The expiration of the statute of limitations is a particularly significant factor in the interest of justice analysis. When the limitations period has run, a dismissal for failure to timely serve process is not simply a procedural setback — it is the permanent extinguishment of the plaintiff's right to litigate the claim. The plaintiff cannot refile. There is no second chance. In such cases, courts have recognized that the interest of justice weighs more heavily in favor of extension, because the alternative is not delay but complete deprivation of the plaintiff's day in court.

Notice to the defendant is another critical factor. If a defendant was aware of the litigation — through informal communication, service attempts that fell short of technical completion, or other means — the argument that the defendant will suffer prejudice from an extension is substantially weaker. A defendant who has been on notice of the action cannot claim to have been blindsided by it, and the policy interest in protecting defendants from being sued long after events occurred is less pressing when the defendant has known about the suit all along.

The merits of the underlying claim are relevant because the interest of justice extension is not a technicality — it is a substantive determination that the case has sufficient merit to justify the extension of procedural indulgence. Courts are not obligated to breathe life into manifestly meritless claims simply because the plaintiff missed a service deadline. But when a plaintiff presents a colorable claim — particularly a claim under a protective statute like the Human Rights Law — the interest of justice factor weighs in favor of allowing the case to proceed on its merits rather than being terminated at the threshold.

What Justice Ondrovic Did and Why It Was Wrong

Justice Ondrovic, in his November 2024 rulings, granted the defendants' motion to dismiss Kane's complaint under CPLR 306-b and denied Kane's cross-motion for an extension of time to serve. The effect was the dismissal of the entire action. Kane would be left without any avenue to pursue his retaliation claims against the Mount Pleasant Central School District.

The Second Department reversed. The appellate panel found that Justice Ondrovic had erred in denying the extension of time to serve and in granting the motion to dismiss. The reversal was issued "on the law and in the exercise of discretion" — language that tracks closely with the standard applicable to CPLR 306-b determinations and that signals the appellate court's finding that the trial court misapplied both the legal standards governing the interest of justice inquiry and its discretionary judgment in failing to extend time.

While the Second Department's decision does not set out a lengthy written opinion detailing every respect in which Justice Ondrovic erred — New York Appellate Division decisions are often terse, particularly in summary dispositions — the "on the law and in the exercise of discretion" formulation tells practitioners familiar with appellate practice what happened: the court found a legal error in the application of the 306-b framework, not merely a discretionary call that could have gone either way.

The most likely basis for reversal, consistent with the Second Department's established jurisprudence under 306-b and the Leader factors, is that Justice Ondrovic failed to adequately engage with the interest of justice balancing test. Courts that grant 306-b dismissals without making findings on the full range of relevant factors — including the prejudice to the plaintiff from dismissal given the potential expiration of limitations periods, the strength of the underlying claim, and the degree of notice the defendant had already received — produce decisions that cannot withstand appellate scrutiny. A mechanical dismissal that does not reflect genuine engagement with the balancing inquiry is not an exercise of discretion; it is an abdication of the judicial role that the statute assigns.

The consequence of Ondrovic's error was not merely procedural inconvenience. His dismissal threatened to permanently extinguish Kane's right to pursue his retaliation claims. If the statute of limitations had expired — which is the factual circumstance that most powerfully invokes the interest of justice extension — Ondrovic's ruling would have meant that Kane could never refile and never have a court evaluate the merits of his allegations. That is precisely the kind of outcome the interest of justice safety valve was designed to prevent.

The Procedural Posture: Appeals from Decisions vs. Appeals from Orders

There is a technical aspect of the Kane reversal that deserves attention because it reveals another dimension of Justice Ondrovic's errors in this case. The Second Department's ruling addressed appeals from three different papers: two decisions dated November 19, 2024, and an order dated November 22, 2024.

The court dismissed the appeals from the two November 19 decisions, noting that no appeal lies from a decision — citing the well-established rule from Schicchi v. J.A. Green Construction Corp., 100 AD2d 509. This is a fundamental principle of New York appellate practice: a "decision" by a court is not itself an appealable paper. Only an "order" — a directive from the court that can be enforced — is appealable. A decision is the court's reasoning; an order is the court's command. Parties who seek to challenge a trial court's ruling must appeal the order, not the decision that preceded it.

The fact that multiple papers were generated in connection with Justice Ondrovic's rulings — two decisions on November 19 and an order on November 22 — reflects the ordinary workflow of trial court proceedings, where a judge will issue a written explanation of the ruling (the decision) and then a formal directive implementing it (the order). The appellate court properly dismissed the appeals from the decisions and reached the merits only with respect to the order.

But the substance of the reversal is fully addressed by the appeal from the November 22 order. The court reversed that order on the law and in the exercise of discretion, denied the defendants' motion to dismiss, and granted Kane's cross-motion to extend the time for service. These are substantive rulings that restore Kane's action and give him the opportunity to proceed with his retaliation claim.

The handling of the multiple papers in Kane is a small illustration of a larger truth about civil litigation: procedural precision matters, and parties and their counsel must navigate the technical requirements of appellate practice correctly in order to obtain the substantive review they seek. Kane's counsel correctly identified the November 22 order as the appealable paper and appealed from it, enabling the Second Department to reach the merits of the 306-b ruling.

Employment Retaliation and the Stakes for Workers

Stepping back from the procedural details, it is worth appreciating the human and institutional stakes in a case like Kane. Employment retaliation is one of the most pervasive and damaging forms of workplace misconduct. Employees who report discrimination, file complaints with regulatory agencies, or refuse to participate in unlawful practices put themselves at professional risk every time they exercise these rights. The fear of retaliation is, for many workers, a powerful deterrent to reporting legitimate grievances — which is exactly why anti-retaliation provisions like §296 of the New York Human Rights Law exist and why they are vigorously enforced.

Public schools and school districts occupy a particular position in this legal landscape. They are public employers, funded by taxpayers, and charged with the important public mission of educating children. When a school district is alleged to have retaliated against an employee for engaging in protected activity, the institutional stakes extend beyond the individual plaintiff. School employees — teachers, administrators, support staff — work in environments where reporting misconduct can be professionally perilous. The accountability mechanisms that anti-retaliation law provides are especially important in institutional settings where power differentials are pronounced and workplace culture can either support or suppress the reporting of wrongdoing.

Christopher Kane's lawsuit alleged that the Mount Pleasant Central School District violated the Human Rights Law's prohibition on retaliation. Whether that allegation would ultimately be sustained on the merits — whether a court or jury would find that the district's conduct crossed the line that §296 draws — is a question that Justice Ondrovic's dismissal prevented from ever being answered. The Second Department's reversal ensures that the question can now be asked and answered in the appropriate forum: a court that evaluates the merits, not one that disposes of the case on a procedural technicality.

Mount Pleasant Central School District: The Institutional Respondent

The Mount Pleasant Central School District is a public school district in Westchester County, New York, serving students in the area surrounding the incorporated village of Pleasantville. Like school districts throughout New York State, it is a government entity subject to the full range of state and federal employment laws, including the New York Human Rights Law. The district and its officials were represented in the appellate proceedings by McGivney, Kluger, Clark & Intoccia, P.C., with Gary J. Intoccia and Mindy Kallus appearing for the respondents.

School districts in New York occupy a legally significant position as employers. They are "public employers" subject to the Taylor Law in collective bargaining matters, and they are covered entities under both the state and federal anti-discrimination laws. Employees of school districts who believe they have been discriminated against or retaliated against for protected activity have multiple potential avenues for redress, including the New York Human Rights Law, the federal Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, and (depending on the nature of the protected activity) whistleblower statutes and constitutional claims under 42 U.S.C. §1983.

The Mount Pleasant Central School District's defense of the dismissal order in the Second Department was unsuccessful. The appellate court's decision denying the defendants' motion to dismiss and granting Kane's extension of time to serve means that the district must now answer Kane's complaint on its merits. The procedural shield that Justice Ondrovic's order provided has been removed. The litigation will proceed.

Justice Ondrovic's Pattern of Procedural Errors in Civil Cases

The Kane reversal is significant on its own terms. But when it is considered alongside the reversal in Jacobson v. Jacobson and other appellate determinations involving Justice Ondrovic, it contributes to a pattern that the public and the bar are entitled to understand.

The pattern that emerges from Justice Ondrovic's reversals is not one of random, isolated misjudgments of the kind that affect every judge at some point in a career. Instead, it appears to be a pattern of failures in the application of multi-factor balancing tests — the kind of judicial analysis that requires courts to engage with a range of considerations, weigh competing interests, and produce findings that reflect that engagement. In matrimonial cases, the failure involves the financial disparity test under Domestic Relations Law §237. In civil cases, the failure involves the interest of justice balancing under CPLR 306-b. The category of error is different in each context, but the underlying failure of judicial method is similar: orders that do not reflect the analytical depth that the applicable legal standards require.

This is a particularly consequential form of judicial error because multi-factor balancing tests exist precisely in situations where the legislature and the courts have determined that mechanical rules would produce unjust outcomes. They are safety valves built into the legal system to allow judges to temper hard-edged procedural requirements with equitable judgment. When a judge fails to apply these safety valves correctly — when the balancing analysis is cursory or absent, when required factors are not considered, when the result does not reflect genuine engagement with the equitable considerations the law demands — the consequence is often severe for the party on the receiving end of the ruling.

In Kane, the consequence was the near-permanent extinction of an employment retaliation claim. In Jacobson, the consequence was a $45,000 extraction from a divorcing wife without the legal foundation that the statute requires. In each case, the Second Department stepped in to correct the error. But the correction came at the cost of appellate proceedings that the aggrieved parties had to finance and prosecute — proceedings that would have been unnecessary had the trial court applied the law correctly in the first instance.

The Role of Appellate Counsel in Correcting Systemic Error

The attorneys who litigated the appeals in both Kane and Jacobson performed an important function that extends beyond advocacy for their individual clients. By pursuing appellate correction of erroneous trial court rulings, they contributed to the body of precedent that constrains future judicial error and ensures that legal standards are articulated and enforced throughout the court system.

In Kane, Kevin T. Mulhearn of Orangeburg, New York, represented the appellant Christopher Kane. Mulhearn faced the task of persuading the Second Department that Justice Ondrovic's 306-b ruling was reversible — a task that required demonstrating both legal error and the concrete equitable harm that flowed from the dismissal. The Second Department's agreement with his position is a vindication of his client's rights and, more broadly, of the principle that employment retaliation claims deserve to be heard on their merits rather than dismissed on procedural grounds when the law provides mechanisms for extensions.

In Jacobson, Ian Steinberg of Berkman Bottger Newman & Schein LLP represented appellant Dana Jacobson. The successful reversal of the $45,000 interim counsel fee award demonstrates that the financial disparity test is not merely theoretical — that it has teeth, and that appellate courts will enforce it when trial courts fail to apply it correctly.

These are the individual stories that collectively constitute the process by which appellate courts maintain the integrity of the legal standards they are charged with enforcing. The law is not a static text that interprets itself. It requires advocates to bring cases to courts that can articulate and apply it, and it requires courts at every level to do their part faithfully. When trial courts fall short, appellate counsel and appellate courts provide the correction. The system, at its best, is self-correcting.

The Broader Implications for Civil Rights Litigants

The Kane reversal has implications that extend beyond the specific parties and the specific procedural question decided. It speaks to the vulnerability of civil rights claims — including Human Rights Law claims — to procedural dismissal at the trial court level, and the critical importance of appellate review in preserving those claims when trial courts err.

Employment discrimination and retaliation claims are among the most procedurally challenging categories of civil litigation. They often involve complex factual records, powerful institutional defendants with substantial resources, and legal standards that require careful judicial engagement. Plaintiffs in employment cases frequently lack the resources to pursue extended litigation and may be represented by counsel working on contingency, who have both a professional interest in moving cases forward and a financial interest in avoiding the additional costs of prolonged procedural battles.

When a trial court dismisses an employment case on a procedural ground that the appellate court subsequently finds to have been erroneously applied, the plaintiff bears real costs that cannot be fully recovered through the appellate process. The time consumed, the uncertainty endured, the potential impact on the plaintiff's ability to secure other employment during the pendency of the litigation — these are harms that flow from the erroneous dismissal and that are not fully remedied by reversal on appeal. The reversal restores the case; it does not restore the time and resources consumed by the unnecessary appellate battle.

For organizations and advocates concerned with employment rights, the Kane case is a reminder of the importance of appellate vigilance. Procedural dismissals of Human Rights Law claims — whether under CPLR 306-b, CPLR 3211, or other provisions — must be challenged when the legal basis for dismissal is questionable. The alternative is allowing cases with legitimate merits to be extinguished at the threshold, which undermines the enforcement of the substantive rights that the Human Rights Law is designed to protect.

Westchester County and the Administration of Justice

Westchester County is one of the most densely litigated counties in New York State outside of the five boroughs of New York City. The Westchester County Supreme Court processes a substantial volume of civil, matrimonial, and criminal matters each year, and the judges who sit in that court affect the lives of an enormous number of people in the Hudson Valley region.

The quality of judicial decision-making in Westchester County matters, therefore, not in the abstract but in the most concrete and immediate sense — it affects whether employees can seek redress for workplace retaliation, whether divorcing spouses receive fair financial treatment, whether businesses and individuals can depend on consistent application of procedural rules that govern their access to the courts. When a judge consistently makes reversible errors in identifiable categories of cases, the impact is felt across the full range of litigants who appear in that judge's courtroom.

The accumulated reversals from Justice Ondrovic's chambers — in matrimonial proceedings, in civil employment cases, across multiple areas of practice — are a matter of public record. They are documented in the decisions of the Appellate Division, Second Department, which are accessible through the New York State unified court system's online legal research resources. Litigants, their counsel, and the public are entitled to know about this record and to draw appropriate conclusions from it.

What those conclusions are, and what if any institutional responses are warranted, are questions that go beyond the scope of what any single article can resolve. But the reporting of the factual record — the compilation of what the appellate courts have said about Justice Ondrovic's rulings, in case after case — is a prerequisite to any meaningful evaluation. The Kane reversal is another entry in that record, another data point that the public is entitled to consider in forming its assessment of judicial accountability in Westchester County.

The Significance of the Argued Appeal

A technical note about the Kane appeal is worth including for its procedural significance. The appeal was argued before the Second Department on September 2, 2025, and decided on October 8, 2025. The fact that the appeal was argued — rather than submitted on the papers — indicates that the appellate court viewed the case as sufficiently important or legally complex to warrant oral argument.

Not all appeals in the Second Department are argued. Many are submitted on the written briefs, with the court resolving them without hearing from counsel in person. When the court orders argument, it typically does so because the legal questions presented are not fully resolved by the existing precedents, because the factual record requires clarification, or because the panel believes that counsel's presentation will assist in reaching the correct result. The grant of argument in Kane suggests that the Second Department recognized the substantive importance of the CPLR 306-b question and the need for a careful, considered decision.

The resulting reversal — issued five weeks after argument — is the product of this deliberate process. It is not a summary disposition issued without meaningful consideration. It is a considered judgment by a four-justice panel, after briefing and argument, that Justice Ondrovic's rulings were reversible errors of law and discretion. The weight of that judgment is commensurate with the process that produced it.

What the Reversal Means Going Forward

The practical effect of the Second Department's reversal in Kane is straightforward: Christopher Kane's retaliation lawsuit against the Mount Pleasant Central School District is restored. The defendants' motion to dismiss under CPLR 306-b has been denied. Kane's cross-motion to extend time to serve has been granted. The case will now proceed in the trial court on whatever schedule the parties and the court establish.

What happens next is uncertain. Employment retaliation cases can resolve through settlement, through summary judgment motions that address the merits of the claims, or through trial. The road ahead for Christopher Kane in this litigation is long, and the outcome is not predetermined. But he now has the opportunity — which Justice Ondrovic's ruling would have permanently foreclosed — to have his claims adjudicated on their merits.

For the Mount Pleasant Central School District, the reversal means that the protective shield of the 306-b dismissal has been removed. The district must now engage with the substance of Kane's allegations. Its attorneys at McGivney, Kluger, Clark & Intoccia must prepare a defense to the merits of the Human Rights Law retaliation claim that Ondrovic's ruling never required them to address. The institutional calculus for the district — whether to continue litigating, whether to seek resolution, what the exposure looks like on the merits — has been reset by the appellate court's decision.

A Note on Judicial Performance and Public Accountability

The Ethics Reporter's examination of Justice Ondrovic's reversal record is undertaken in the public interest and in the tradition of investigative legal journalism that takes seriously the proposition that courts are accountable to the public they serve. Judges in New York are public officials. Their decisions are the exercise of public power, and the quality of that exercise is a matter of legitimate public concern.

The accumulated record of reversals from Justice Ondrovic's chambers — documented in published appellate decisions that are themselves public records — raises questions that the public is entitled to ask and that the legal profession is entitled to examine. Not every reversal reflects a serious failing; the appellate system is designed to catch and correct trial court errors, and the existence of such a system implies that errors will occur. But a pattern of reversals, in identifiable categories of cases, involving the same types of analytical failures, raises more pointed questions about the consistency and quality of judicial decision-making in a specific courtroom.

Those questions are not answered by pointing to the existence of the appellate system. The appellate system corrects errors in the cases that come before it. It does not compensate the litigants who were harmed by the original error and who either lacked the resources to appeal or were not made whole by the appellate correction. It does not address the systemic effect of a pattern of error on the parties who appeared in the affected courtroom and never knew that the rulings they received were legally infirm. And it does not, by itself, ensure that the judge whose rulings were reversed has absorbed the appellate correction and modified his approach accordingly.

These are the questions that judicial accountability mechanisms — the Commission on Judicial Conduct, the electoral process, the inherent supervisory authority of the Chief Administrative Judge — exist to address. Whether those mechanisms are being used to engage with the record of reversals from Justice Ondrovic's chambers is a question that the public, the bar, and the institutions responsible for judicial oversight are best positioned to answer.

Conclusion: Restoration of a Right

Christopher Kane went to court to hold his employer accountable for alleged retaliation under New York's Human Rights Law. A Westchester County Supreme Court judge dismissed his case on a procedural ground — failure to timely serve process — without properly applying the balancing test that the law requires for that determination. The Appellate Division, Second Department reversed the dismissal, denied the defendants' motion, and granted Kane an extension of time to complete service.

The story of Kane v. Mount Pleasant Central School District is, at its core, a story about access to justice — about whether the procedural gates of the court system are opened or closed to a worker who alleges that he was retaliated against for exercising protected rights. Justice Ondrovic closed the gate. The Second Department opened it again.

But the case is also a story about a pattern of judicial error that has now been documented across multiple areas of law and multiple categories of litigants in Westchester County Supreme Court. That pattern deserves the attention of the bar, the press, and the public. The cases that were appealed and corrected are the visible record. The cases that were not appealed — the orders that became final without correction — represent a shadow population of potential errors that the public record cannot fully illuminate.

Judicial accountability requires both the correction of individual errors and the evaluation of patterns. The Appellate Division, Second Department has done its part in Kane and in Jacobson. The question of whether the broader accountability mechanisms are functioning as they should — whether the pattern of reversals from Justice Ondrovic's chambers is receiving the institutional attention it warrants — is a question that this publication will continue to examine.
