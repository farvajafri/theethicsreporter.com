When Lawyers Become Their Own Judges: The Breakdown of Legal Ethics in the Age of Outrage

On July 28, 2020, Paul Alexander Clark, a Jersey City attorney admitted to the bar in 2012, drove his part-time employee to the home of a Superior Court judge presiding over one of his cases. He did not attempt to serve the judge in a conventional manner—at his office, at a courthouse, through proper channels. Instead, according to the New Jersey Supreme Court's disciplinary decision, filed just this week, Clark hand-delivered court documents to the judge's residence in what the court would later characterize as a deliberate effort to intimidate. The judge, when he realized what had happened, felt he was in "real danger." He called himself "scared" for himself and his family.

Two days later, Clark appeared on a podcast. He admitted to driving to the judge's home. He called him a "little petty tyrant." He said the judge was using his influence to "lash out at people he dislikes," employing "cronies" in the New Jersey State Police as enforcers. Then he named the town where the judge lived—unnecessary information that served no purpose except to amplify whatever threat had already been made.

In other podcast appearances, over a decade-long divorce case that produced more than 110 court orders and five different judges, Clark went further. He encouraged listeners to "follow judges" and "watch their extracurricular activities, because that's the only way to get to them." He compared one judge to Hitler, Stalin, and Saddam Hussein. He alleged another was "the head of a child predator/Mafia ring." He filed federal lawsuits against judges; he sought their recusal; he relitigated matters already decided. His client filed social media posts suggesting the judge "masterminded an evil kidnapping, abuse and psychological murder" of his children—children the client had not seen in years.

On Friday, May 9, 2026—one day ago—the New Jersey Supreme Court Disciplinary Review Board imposed an indefinite suspension on Clark, effective immediately. He is not eligible to petition for reinstatement for at least four years. Before he can return to practice, he must complete a continuing legal education course in ethics and professionalism and submit to the court proof of his fitness to practice, as attested by a medical doctor. The court found that Clark had violated the Rules of Professional Conduct by engaging in frivolous litigation, making statements with reckless disregard for the truth about a judge, and engaging in conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice.

Clark disputes the characterization entirely. In interviews after the decision, he framed his suspension not as the inevitable consequence of a lawyer's decisions but as an assault on free speech. "The states and their ethics committees have been using these types of things in a variety of states to try and stifle attorneys who criticize the government," he said. "I'm not the only one that's happened to." He plans to petition the U.S. Supreme Court, arguing that his suspension violates his First Amendment rights and denies him due process.

The Clark decision arrived on the heels of another, more opaque moment in the American legal system's reckoning with professional conduct. Three days earlier, on May 7, the New York Times reported that the Special Counsel's office was investigating a Trump administration lawyer for possible misconduct following testimony in federal court. A judge presiding over the case, according to reporting, had called what the lawyer said a "serious breakdown" in legal ethics and "a concerning departure from normal practice." The judge asked for a broader inquiry into possible violations of the rules governing lawyers.

Both cases hinge on the same question: What constitutes a violation of professional ethics? And more urgently, who gets to decide?

The answer, in 2026, appears to be: it depends.

In the opening months of this year, American courts imposed more than $145,000 in sanctions on lawyers for fabricating citations using artificial intelligence. These were not lawyers who misunderstood a rule. They were lawyers who submitted briefs containing invented case names, fabricated quotations, and nonexistent legal authorities. A winery dispute in Oregon netted a $15,000 sanction when the court found fifteen fake citations and eight invented quotations in the lawyer's briefs. A Sixth Circuit case involving fireworks resulted in a $15,000 sanction per lawyer—and an order requiring them to pay the other side's appellate fees and double costs—after the court identified more than two dozen wrong, misleading, or nonexistent citations.

These are not abstract violations. When a lawyer submits a false citation to a court, real judges spend time investigating the phantom case. Real opposing counsel must disprove something that was never written. Real courts are forced to second-guess decisions made in reliance on authorities that do not exist. The sanctions represent, in some sense, the court's attempt to price the harm in dollars and hours.

But here is where the story fractures: according to a Northwestern University survey published this spring, sixty-one point six percent of federal judges report using artificial intelligence tools in their judicial work. The majority of them use AI for legal research and document review—the exact same tasks that, when performed by lawyers, have attracted escalating sanctions if a single fact is wrong.

No federal judge has yet faced a sanction for an AI error in an opinion, a research memo, or a document review. No judge has been ordered to pay the other side's fees for relying on a hallucinated case. No judge's error has resulted in a call for further inquiry into possible ethical violations. The gap between what courts demand of lawyers and what courts tolerate in themselves has become a chasm.

This is not a minor inconsistency. It is the erosion of the principle that legal ethics apply equally, or ought to. It is the announcement, in sanctions stacked upon sanctions, that the legal profession has embraced a two-tier system of accountability: one for those who argue before the court, and another for those who sit in judgment.

The Paul Alexander Clark decision is instructive precisely because it offers no artificial intelligence to hide behind. Clark did not fabricate a citation. He did not rely on a machine that had promised to be smarter than he was. He made calculated choices. He drove to a judge's house. He appeared on podcasts. He encouraged his listeners to surveil a jurist. He claimed things he could not prove. These were not failures of technology; they were failures of judgment, restraint, and professional honor.

Yet Clark's defense—that his suspension represents an assault on his First Amendment rights—cuts to something real. Lawyers do have the right to criticize judges. The First Amendment is not suspended at the courthouse door. Bar associations cannot use ethics rules as a weapon to silence legitimate dissent. The New Jersey Supreme Court, in its decision, labored to distinguish between speech that criticizes a judge's legal reasoning and conduct that is intended to intimidate, harass, or endanger. Clark's conduct, the court found, crossed that line decisively. The tone of that crossing—from speech to threat—is what made it unethical.

Where the line sits, however, remains contested. Clark's own language in his interviews suggests that he and the court are speaking past each other. He sees his suspension as the elimination of his voice. The court sees his conduct as the elimination of a judge's safety. Both frames are, in some sense, truthful. Both contain a legitimate claim to harm. The tragedy of the Clark case is not that one side is right and the other wrong, but that the legal profession has lost any mechanism for holding both truths at once.

The Trump administration lawyer's case, by contrast, offers a view of professional misconduct that is more conventionally understood as unethical but less clearly articulated. The Times reported that the lawyer had testified before a federal court in a manner that prompted the presiding judge to remark on a "serious breakdown" in legal ethics. But the newspaper's account did not specify what the lawyer said, or whether he misrepresented facts, misquoted testimony, or committed some other breach of the advocate's basic duty to be truthful with the court. The Special Counsel's investigation is ongoing. The facts are not public. Yet already the case has become a Rorschach test, with partisans on both sides projecting their own suspicions about the Trump administration's legal tactics.

What binds these cases together is not the specific conduct in question but the loss of clarity about what lawyers owe to the system they serve. For decades, the ideal of legal ethics was built on a simple foundation: lawyers are officers of the court. They have a duty not just to their clients but to the judicial system itself. That duty required candor with the court, good faith in disputes with opposing counsel, and a recognition that the law itself—the preservation of the courts, the integrity of the legal process—was a client owed allegiance that sometimes superseded the interest of a paying customer.

That ideal has eroded. In its place, the legal profession has substituted a kind of ethical tribalism. Lawyers view ethics rules through the lens of partisan interest. A Democrat sees Paul Alexander Clark as a rogue; a Republican sees him as a voice silenced by judicial overreach. A Republican sees the Trump administration lawyer as persecuted; a Democrat sees him as slipping the bonds of truth. The courts, meanwhile, enforce ethics rules with increasing severity against lawyers while exempting themselves from the same scrutiny. The AI sanctions represent the court's attempt to reassert control over the profession through escalating financial punishment, yet that very move exposes the inconsistency of a system in which judges use the same technologies without consequence.

The bar associations, which once functioned as the custodians of professional norms, have largely ceded that role. They issue ethics opinions; they train lawyers in compliance; but they lack the stomach for genuine disciplinary action. Paul Alexander Clark was "admitted to the New Jersey bar in 2012 and had not faced prior discipline" before this case, despite a decade-long trail of conduct that by any reasonable standard violated professional conduct rules. It took a judge feeling frightened in his own home, it took podcasts naming his address, it took a federal appearance that prompted judicial alarm, for the ethics apparatus to move. The system is not designed to prevent misconduct; it is designed to punish it publicly after it has already caused harm.

The consequence is that legal ethics in 2026 has become something other than a shared standard. It is a series of isolated episodes in which the powerful and the powerless are held to radically different standards. A judge can use artificial intelligence in opinions without the validation of a single peer review. A lawyer cannot use it in a brief without risking tens of thousands of dollars in sanctions. A judge can privately worry about his safety because of a lawyer's conduct, but the legal system has no mechanism for holding lawyers accountable unless that conduct publicly violates a specific rule. A lawyer can claim that he is being silenced by ethics enforcement, and the claim will resonate with half the profession, because the ethics system has lost any apparent grounding in shared principle.

What is needed, plainly, is not more enforcement but a restoration of coherence. The courts must apply the same technology standards to themselves as they apply to lawyers. Bar associations must treat the early signs of ethical decay—the frivolous filing, the intemperate podcast, the casual allegation—as occasions for mentorship and course correction rather than waiting for catastrophe before acting. Lawyers must abandon the fiction that ethics rules are political weapons and recognize them as the minimum baseline for a profession that claims to serve justice. And the legal system as a whole must rebuild the idea that professional ethics are not a burden imposed on lawyers by judges, but a reciprocal obligation that binds everyone who wears the robe or carries the briefcase.

Paul Alexander Clark will not practice law for at least four years. He will not represent clients. He will not appear in court. In his place, there will be other lawyers, some of whom will use artificial intelligence to research his case, relying on machines that may or may not have bothered to verify the cases they cite. Some of them may even be judges, using the same technology to draft opinions that will bind his fate. The system that has chosen to suspend him will meanwhile conduct its own business with fewer constraints, less verification, and no public reckoning. That Clark's suspension arrives in an age of such contradictions suggests not that he should have been forgiven, but that the legal profession's idea of ethics has fractured beyond recognition.
