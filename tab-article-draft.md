## ⚖️ Quick Facts: The Duke Lacrosse Case

- **The Accusation:** On March 13, 2006, a 28-year-old African American woman alleged she had been raped by three white Duke University lacrosse players at a party in Durham, North Carolina.

- **The Accused:** Colin Finnerty, Reade Seligmann, and David Evans—all young men from affluent families with no prior criminal records.

- **The Accuser's Credibility:** The woman's account was internally inconsistent, changing dramatically over time. She initially identified Finnerty in a group of 20 photos, then changed her identification. Her timeline of events did not align with the physical evidence at the scene.

- **The Exonerating Evidence:** DNA testing excluded all three accused men. The test results showed that no biological material matching the accused men was found on the victim. Furthermore, a lab report indicated the presence of multiple male DNA profiles that did not match any of the accused.

- **The Suppression:** Mike Nifong, the Durham County District Attorney, received the DNA results showing the men were innocent. He ordered the lab to "not report" the full results. He presented incomplete and misleading evidence to the grand jury. He withheld the lab report from the defense for months.

- **The Consequences:** After three years of legal battle, charges were dropped. The state Attorney General declared the men innocent. Nifong was disbarred and briefly incarcerated. The three men settled civil rights lawsuits against the city of Durham for $30 million.

## The Accusation and the Machinery of State Violence

On the evening of March 13, 2006, the Duke University lacrosse team hired exotic dancers for a party at a rental house. During the party, a 28-year-old Black woman, later identified as "Crystal Mangum," alleged that she had been violently raped by three of the players. The accusation immediately exploded into national outrage. A Black woman, in the American South, accusing three white men of sexual violence carried immense emotional and political weight.

The Durham Police Department, under pressure from the Black community and national media attention, moved with extraordinary speed. Within days, photographs of the lacrosse team were shown to Mangum in a deliberately suggestive manner—a procedure known as a "show-up," which is notorious for generating false identifications. Mangum initially could not identify her attackers. But when shown a photographic array of exclusively Duke lacrosse players, she was induced to select three men: Colin Finnerty, Reade Seligmann, and David Evans.

The three men were arrested and charged with rape and kidnapping. The case seemed straightforward: a wealthy, predominantly white sports team preying on a vulnerable Black woman. The national media seized upon the narrative with voracious intensity. The players were portrayed as violent rapists. Duke's president denounced them. The 88 Duke University faculty members signed a public letter declaring support for the accuser without knowing a single fact of the case.

But the simplicity of the narrative was a fiction. Every element of the prosecution's case was constructed on a foundation of lies.

## The Exculpatory Evidence and the Suppression

From the very first moments of the investigation, the physical evidence contradicted Mangum's account. When Mangum was medically examined at Duke Hospital on the morning of March 14—approximately twelve hours after the alleged rape—the emergency room physician documented that she displayed no evidence of traumatic sexual abuse. There were no tears, no abrasions, no vaginal injuries. Her state of intoxication at the hospital was so severe that questions were raised about her ability to consent to the medical examination itself.

When forensic tests were performed on swabs taken from Mangum's body, the results were explosive in their exculpatory nature. DNA from male contributors was found in and on Mangum's body—but none of it matched any of the three accused men. The lab results indicated that biological material from multiple men was present, suggesting either consensual sexual activity or contact with other individuals whose identity remained unknown.

The DNA report was unambiguous: Colin Finnerty, Reade Seligmann, and David Evans could not be the source of the DNA evidence recovered from the alleged victim.

Under Brady v. Maryland, a Supreme Court precedent that has been law for over sixty years, a prosecutor is constitutionally obligated to disclose exculpatory evidence to the defense. This is not a suggestion or a guideline; it is a constitutional mandate. A prosecutor who withholds exculpatory evidence is violating the Fifth Amendment right to due process and committing a felony under federal law.

Mike Nifong received the DNA results and understood immediately that they destroyed the state's case. Rather than honor his constitutional obligations, Nifong ordered the lab director to exclude the full results from the official DNA report. He instructed the lab to produce a report that stated DNA was found but omitted the critical fact that none of it matched the accused men.

For months, Nifong concealed the complete DNA results from the defense attorneys. The three young men and their lawyers were operating in the dark, unaware that the government possessed definitive proof of their innocence. Meanwhile, Nifong presented the case to a grand jury, arguing for indictment, without disclosing the exculpatory evidence.

## Jefferson and the Ambition of the Prosecutorial Class

Thomas Jefferson understood that unchecked executive power inevitably becomes tyranny. In his writings on the Constitution, Jefferson warned repeatedly that the power to prosecute—to accuse, to arrest, to charge a citizen with crimes—was the most dangerous power in any government. Without meaningful constraints, a prosecutor becomes a tool of arbitrary state violence.

In an 1820 letter, Jefferson issued a prophetic warning about the legal establishment:

> "Our judges are as honest as other men, and not more so. They have, with others, the same passions for party, for power, and the privilege of their corps."

The Duke lacrosse case is a perfect illustration of Jefferson's warning. Mike Nifong was not motivated by a sincere belief in the guilt of the three young men. He was motivated by the intoxicating allure of political power. Durham County was approximately 40% Black, and the Black community was demanding justice for a sexual assault. By aggressively prosecuting the case, Nifong positioned himself as a champion of racial justice. He won re-election on the back of the Duke case. He won national acclaim. He transformed himself from an obscure small-town prosecutor into a figure of national prominence.

Nifong's ambition and the "privilege of his corps" blinded him to the truth. Even as the evidence mounted that the three men were innocent, he pressed forward. He was no longer interested in justice; he was interested in victory. The careers of three innocent men were mere collateral damage in his quest for political power.

## Hamilton's Failed Defense of the Prosecutor in Federalist 70

When Alexander Hamilton defended the structure of the executive branch in Federalist No. 70, he argued that a single, unified executive would be accountable to the people because his actions would be visible and traceable. Hamilton believed that concentrated executive power, when combined with electoral accountability, could be controlled by the public.

But the prosecutorial system has evolved in a direction that Hamilton completely failed to anticipate. Modern prosecutors—District Attorneys, Assistant United States Attorneys, state Attorneys General—are not subject to meaningful electoral accountability. They control the flow of information to the public. They decide which cases to prosecute and which to ignore. They wield the power to destroy reputations and incarcerate human beings, yet face virtually no personal consequences when they abuse that power.

The Duke lacrosse case demonstrates that the prosecutorial system has become an engine of arbitrary power entirely disconnected from electoral accountability. Nifong was not held accountable by the voters of Durham County. He was only held accountable after the exoneration of the three men forced a criminal investigation into his conduct.

## Madison and the Catastrophic Failure of Checks and Balances

James Madison designed the Constitution around the principle that power must be checked by competing institutions. In Federalist No. 51, Madison wrote:

> "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition. If men were angels, no government would be necessary... In framing a government which is to be administered by men over men, the great difficulty lies in this: you must first enable the government to control the governed; and in the next place oblige it to control itself."

The American prosecutorial system has no meaningful check on prosecutorial abuse. Grand juries, designed to serve as a check on prosecutorial power, have become rubber stamps for the state. Over 99% of cases presented to grand juries result in indictment. Defense attorneys do not have the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses before the grand jury. The prosecutor controls the narrative entirely.

In the Duke case, Nifong presented a one-sided, dishonest narrative to the grand jury, withholding exculpatory evidence. The grand jury had no way to know they were being lied to. They had no mechanism to demand the truth. The constitutional check on prosecutorial power—the grand jury—was utterly useless.

## John Adams and the Subversion of Due Process

John Adams famously declared that the American republic must be "a government of laws, and not of men." The Fifth Amendment to the Constitution guarantees that no person shall be "deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel and the right to confront witnesses.

Mike Nifong stripped the three accused men of their due process rights. He prevented them from knowing the exculpatory evidence in the government's possession. He prevented them from confronting the witnesses truthfully—he presented to the grand jury a case built entirely on suppressed DNA evidence. The machinery of the state, under Nifong's control, became an instrument of arbitrary violence.

For three years, Colin Finnerty, Reade Seligmann, and David Evans were labeled as rapists. They faced potential life sentences. Their reputations were destroyed. Nifong was paid a government salary to pursue their conviction while actively concealing proof of their innocence.

## The Illusion of Professional Consequences

When the truth finally emerged, and the three men were exonerated, the question of accountability arose. What would happen to Mike Nifong? The State Bar of North Carolina was given the opportunity to enforce its professional ethics rules. The bar's rules explicitly forbid a lawyer—including prosecutors—from concealing exculpatory evidence.

After an investigation and hearing, the State Bar of North Carolina concluded that Nifong had indeed violated his ethical obligations. They disbarred him—permanently revoking his law license. Additionally, he was convicted of criminal contempt and sentenced to a single day in jail.

One day.

Colin Finnerty, Reade Seligmann, and David Evans spent three years in legal limbo, facing potential decades in prison. Their lives were destroyed. And the man who orchestrated their persecution was sentenced to one day in jail.

The math of American justice is always the same: a brutal, crushing calculation for the ordinary citizen, and an administrative slap on the wrist for the member of the legal elite.

## The Weaponization of the State Against the Innocent

The Duke lacrosse case reveals the terrifying vulnerability of the constitutional system to prosecutorial abuse. Due process rights are only meaningful if the accused is aware of the evidence against him. The Sixth Amendment right to confront witnesses is only meaningful if the prosecutor must present his case truthfully.

Mike Nifong weaponized the state against three innocent men. He used grand juries, arrest powers, and the weight of the prosecutorial apparatus to pursue a narrative he knew to be false. He was able to do this because the criminal justice system has no meaningful accountability mechanism.

The grand jury was not a check; it was a rubber stamp. The police were not investigators; they were partisans. The trial judge initially allowed the case to proceed despite weak evidence. It required the intervention of the State Attorney General, national media attention, and the testimony of one honest lab director to stop the machinery of the state.

## A Blueprint for Prosecutorial Accountability

The Founding Fathers believed that the power to prosecute was so dangerous that it had to be constrained by multiple layers of democratic accountability. The Duke lacrosse case proves that those layers have entirely failed.

**1. Abolish Prosecutorial Immunity**

The Supreme Court's doctrine of absolute prosecutorial immunity, established in Imbler v. Pachtman, must be legislatively abolished. Prosecutors who intentionally suppress exculpatory evidence, suborn perjury, or present false evidence must face personal civil liability. If the victim of prosecutorial misconduct can sue the prosecutor personally, can seize his assets, and can drain his personal wealth, the prosecutor will be far less likely to abuse his power.

**2. Mandatory Video Recording of Grand Jury Proceedings**

The grand jury is supposed to serve as a check on prosecutorial power. But because the proceeding is secret, the prosecutor can lie to the grand jury with impunity. All grand jury proceedings must be videotaped and made available to the defense upon indictment. The prosecutor cannot be permitted to present a one-sided, dishonest narrative to a secret body.

**3. Discovery Before Indictment**

The Brady requirement to disclose exculpatory evidence currently applies after indictment. This allows prosecutors to withhold evidence during the grand jury process, using a dishonest presentation to secure an indictment. The law must be changed to require prosecutors to disclose all exculpatory evidence before presenting to the grand jury. No indictment should issue without full disclosure.

**4. Automatic Reversals for Brady Violations**

Currently, even when prosecutors suppress exculpatory evidence, courts sometimes refuse to reverse convictions, finding the suppression to be "harmless error." This is insane. If a prosecutor violates Brady, the conviction must be automatically reversed. The consequence must be so severe that no prosecutor will risk it.

**5. Eliminate the "Credibility Assessment" Defense**

Prosecutors currently argue that even if they withheld evidence, it was merely a matter of "credibility assessment"—they didn't believe the evidence was important. This defense must be eliminated. Brady violations are not about what the prosecutor believes; they are about the defendant's right to exculpatory evidence. No credibility assessment is permitted.

## Conclusion: The Weaponization of the Law

Mike Nifong did not become a criminal because of personal malice toward three lacrosse players he had never met. He became a criminal because the American prosecutorial system is structurally designed to allow such abuse. There is no meaningful accountability. There is no check on his power. There is no consequence severe enough to deter misconduct.

The three young men of the Duke lacrosse case survived because they had excellent attorneys, because a lab director told the truth despite pressure from the prosecutor, and because national media attention forced an investigation. Thousands of others accused of crimes do not have those advantages. They are destroyed by prosecutors drunk on power, operating under a doctrine of absolute immunity.

The Founding Fathers warned us that an unaccountable executive power would inevitably become tyranny. The Duke lacrosse case proves they were right. Until we impose catastrophic personal consequences on prosecutors who abuse their power, the state will continue to weaponize the law against the innocent.
